# 25% Cement Tariffs on Canadian and Mexican Imports Squeeze Supply

## Key Details
- **25% tariff** on cement imports from Canada and Mexico, effective April 5, 2025
- US imports approximately **20%** of total cement consumption
- Canada supplies ~**5 million metric tons** annually
- Mexico supplies ~**2 million metric tons** annually
- Together they account for over **25%** of all US cement imports
- Ready-mix concrete in the US: **$125-$200 per cubic yard** for standard mixes (2026)
- Concrete costs up **3-7%** from 2025
- Concrete and cement still approximately **6%** higher than two years ago

## Regional Impact
- **Northern states** (New York, Washington) hardest hit -- Canadian imports account for up to **36%** of regional consumption
- **Southwest** (Texas, Arizona, California, Florida) -- Mexican imports ~5% of consumption, more modest immediate impact
- New York City concrete averages **$180-$250/cubic yard**
- Texas averages **$110-$165/cubic yard**

## Industry Context
- The US does not produce enough cement domestically to meet demand
- Domestic capacity additions take **3-5 years** to come online
- Portland Cement Association warned that tariffs could create regional shortages
- Greek and Canadian cement producers identified as hardest-hit exporters to the US

## Analysis
- Cement is a critical input for virtually all construction types -- residential foundations, commercial structures, infrastructure, roads, and bridges
- The 25% tariff creates regional supply risk, particularly in northern border states where Canadian cement is essential
- Unlike steel (where domestic capacity can absorb some import displacement), cement plant construction takes years, making near-term supply adjustment difficult
- For building materials companies: domestic cement producers (CRH, Eagle Materials, Summit Materials) benefit from reduced import competition and pricing power
- Infrastructure projects (IIJA-funded) that are cement-intensive (roads, bridges) face cost escalation that could reduce the real purchasing power of federal funding
