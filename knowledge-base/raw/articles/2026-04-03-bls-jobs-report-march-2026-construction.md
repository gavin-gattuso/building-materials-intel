# March 2026 Jobs Report: 178K Payrolls, Construction Adds 26K

## Key Details
- Total nonfarm payroll employment: **+178,000** in March (vs. consensus estimate of 59,000)
- February revised sharply downward: **-133,000** (from original -92,000)
- Unemployment rate: **4.3%** (little changed)
- Labor force participation rate: slipped to **61.9%** from 62.0%
- Average hourly earnings: **+0.2%** month-over-month, **+3.5%** year-over-year (lowest annual gain since May 2021)

## Construction Employment
- Construction added **26,000 jobs** in March
- Construction employment has shown "little net change over the prior 12 months"
- Job gains were spread across specialty trade contractors and heavy construction

## Industry Breakdown
- Health care: **+76,000** (inflated by Kaiser Permanente physicians strike resolution -- ~37,000 returning workers)
- Construction: **+26,000**
- Transportation and warehousing: gains reported
- The Kaiser strike effect means the "real" underlying job creation was closer to ~141,000

## Wage Growth Context
- The **3.5%** annual wage growth is the lowest since May 2021
- Decelerating wage growth could give the Fed more room to cut rates
- However, construction wage growth continues to exceed the national average due to persistent labor shortages

## Analysis
- The headline +178K beat expectations significantly, but the massive February downward revision (-41,000) and Kaiser strike distortions cloud the picture
- Construction's +26K gain is moderate and consistent with an industry growing slowly but steadily
- The labor force participation decline suggests some workers are leaving the workforce, which may tighten the construction labor pool further
- Slowing wage growth across the broader economy contrasts with elevated construction wage growth, highlighting the sector's unique labor dynamics
- For building materials: moderate construction employment growth supports steady demand, but the broader labor market softening could signal future headwinds
