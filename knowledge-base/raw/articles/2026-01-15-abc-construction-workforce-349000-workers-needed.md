# ABC: Construction Industry Must Attract 349,000 Workers in 2026 Despite Macroeconomic Headwinds

## Key Findings

- The construction industry needs an estimated **349,000 net new workers** in 2026 to meet demand
- This is down from **439,000** in 2025 and over **500,000** in each of the two prior years
- More than **half** of the 349,000 workers needed are simply to **replace retirees**, not for incremental growth
- Approximately **20%** of the construction workforce is **over age 55**
- By 2031, **41% of construction workers** are expected to retire (per Deloitte)
- Only **10%** of current workers are **under 25**

## Wage Trends

- Construction wages rising faster than the broader economy
- Tighter immigration policy has added more upward pressure
- Contractors should plan for labor costs to increase **6-8% annually** for 2026 bids
- Construction earnings continue to outpace broader private-sector averages

## Demand Drivers

- Demand for **electricians** has surged due to rapid data center construction
- IIJA infrastructure spending reaching peak execution phase
- CHIPS Act megaprojects requiring specialized labor

## Employment Data (January 2026 BLS Report)

- Construction added **33,000 jobs** in January 2026
- Gain concentrated in **nonresidential specialty trade contractors** (+25,000)
- Follows a year in which the sector saw little employment increase
- Total nonfarm payrolls increased by **130,000** (revised to 160,000)

## Future Outlook

- In 2027, the industry will need **456,000 new workers** as construction spending growth resumes
- The smaller 2026 figure reflects cyclical slowdown, not structural improvement in labor availability
- Demographics, retirements, skills mismatches, and immigration enforcement remain persistent constraints
