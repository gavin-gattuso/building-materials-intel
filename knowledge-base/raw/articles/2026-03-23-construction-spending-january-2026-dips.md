# January 2026 Construction Spending Dips 0.3%, Data Centers Can't Lift Weak Market

## Key Details
- Total construction spending: **$2.19 trillion** SAAR in January 2026
- Month-over-month: **-0.3%** from December
- Nonresidential construction: **$728.2 billion** SAAR, down **0.4%** month-over-month
- Private construction spending: declined **0.6%** for the month, essentially flat year-over-year
- Private residential: declined month-over-month
- Manufacturing construction: down **2%** month-over-month
- Public construction: gains partially offsetting private sector weakness
- Data center spending: rose another **2%** in January -- the standout growth area

## Sector Details
- Declines in **9 of 16** nonresidential categories
- Data centers continue to stand apart as primary area of private-sector expansion
- Manufacturing spending decline may reflect completion of some CHIPS Act-funded projects
- Public construction gains driven by IIJA-funded highway and water infrastructure

## Data Release Delays
- February 2026 data and March 2026 data both rescheduled for release on **May 7, 2026**
- This creates a multi-month visibility gap for construction spending trends

## Analysis
- The 0.3% decline shows the construction industry entering 2026 on a soft footing
- The data center boom (+2%) is real but insufficient to lift the broader market out of weakness
- AGC notes that "rising construction costs and uncertainty over tariffs, war in the Middle East, and a slowing economy are leading to slowdowns and cancellations of many project types"
- For building materials: the bifurcated market (data centers growing, most other sectors flat-to-declining) means material demand depends heavily on product mix and end-market exposure
- Companies exposed to infrastructure/public construction are better positioned than those primarily serving private nonresidential
- The data release delays are an additional headwind for industry planning and forecasting
