# March FOMC: Fed Holds Rates at 3.50-3.75% Amid Iran Conflict Uncertainty

## Key Details
- Federal funds rate held at **3.50% - 3.75%** (unanimous minus one dissent)
- **One dissent:** Fed Gov. Stephen Miran voted for a quarter-point cut
- This is the second consecutive hold after three cuts totaling 75 bps in late 2025
- Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) released -- first of 2026
- Median dot plot: **one rate cut** projected for remainder of 2026
- Inflation forecast: PCE inflation running above the **2% target**

## Summary of Economic Projections (March 2026 SEP)
- Median fed funds rate projection for end of 2026: **3.25-3.50%** (implies one 25bp cut)
- GDP growth projection for 2026: revised modestly lower
- Unemployment rate projection: revised slightly higher
- PCE inflation projection: remains "somewhat elevated" above 2%

## Key Factors in Decision
- **Labor market softening:** unemployment at 4.3%, job gains moderating
- **Iran war uncertainty:** oil price spike from Strait of Hormuz disruption creating inflationary pressure
- **Persistent inflation:** core PCE still running above target, complicated by energy price shock
- **Tariff-driven cost pressures:** contributing to sticky inflation readings

## Market Reaction
- Markets had largely priced in the hold
- CME FedWatch tool shifted probability of June cut to approximately **55%**
- 10-year Treasury yield relatively stable around **4.1-4.2%**
- Mortgage rates ticked up slightly in the week following the announcement

## Analysis
- The Fed is caught between conflicting signals: weakening growth (sub-1% Q4 GDP) argues for cuts, while Iran-driven oil shock and tariff inflation argue for holding
- For construction and building materials, the rate hold means mortgage rates stay elevated (~6%+), limiting housing demand recovery
- The "one cut" dot plot for 2026 is hawkish relative to market hopes for multiple cuts
- Builder sentiment (NAHB HMI at 37-38) reflects the affordability squeeze from sustained high rates
- The longer rates stay elevated, the more pressure on residential construction starts and building permit activity
