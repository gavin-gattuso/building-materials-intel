# ABC: Construction Industry Must Attract 349,000 Workers in 2026

## Key Details
- Estimated net new workers needed in 2026: **349,000** (down from 439,000 in 2025)
- Projected to rise to **456,000** in 2027 as spending growth resumes
- Majority of 2026 demand driven by **retirement** rather than increased demand for services
- Nearly **one-fifth of electricians** are over age 55
- Demand for electricians with precision wiring skills surged due to data center construction

## Root Causes
- Aging workforce: baby boomer retirements accelerating
- Insufficient pipeline of new entrants
- Competition from other sectors (manufacturing, tech) for skilled workers
- Immigration policy uncertainty reducing foreign-born workforce availability

## Industry Response
- Contractors deepening **workforce development initiatives**
- Expanding **apprenticeship pathways**
- Investing in **credentialing programs**
- Prioritizing **retention of senior talent**
- Accelerating technology adoption: digital planning, modular construction, efficient field practices

## Wage Pressure
- ABC Chief Economist Anirban Basu: failure to attract workers "will worsen labor shortages, especially in certain occupations and regions, placing further upward pressure on labor costs"
- Construction wages growing faster than economy-wide average
- Tight labor supply driving increased competition for skilled workers

## Analysis
- The 349,000 figure is lower than 2025's 439,000 partly because macroeconomic headwinds have modestly dampened construction demand growth
- However, the retirement-driven component means the shortage is structural, not cyclical -- it will persist regardless of economic conditions
- For building materials: labor shortages constrain the pace of construction activity (fewer workers = fewer projects completed), which can limit material demand growth even when funding is available
- The electrician shortage is particularly acute and directly connects to the data center construction boom
- Companies investing in modular and prefabricated construction may see accelerating adoption as labor shortages push the industry toward more off-site manufacturing
