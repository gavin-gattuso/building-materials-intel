# Construction's New Worker Demand Drops to 349,000 in 2026 -- But Structural Risks Remain

## Key Findings

- Construction industry needs **349,000 net new workers** in 2026 -- a precipitous drop from previous years
- However, the smaller figure should NOT be seen as a turning point for labor availability
- Indicates a **cyclical slowdown** in construction activity layered on structural workforce limitations

## Workforce Demographics

- More than **half** of the 349,000 workers needed will replace retirees, not support growth
- Approximately **20%** of the construction workforce is over age 55
- Demographics, retirements, and skills mismatches continue to limit the workforce

## Wage Trends

- Average hourly earnings in construction: **$39.70/hour** (mid-2025), rising **3.7% year-on-year**
- Construction salary growth of **8-12%** projected for 2026
- This is more than double the broader US economy's average wage growth of **3.5-4%**
- Labor shortages making bidding riskier with **6-8% annual wage hikes** expected

### Wage by Category
| Role | Annual Salary Range |
|------|-------------------|
| General laborers | $48,000-$52,000 |
| Skilled trades (electricians, plumbers) | $65,000-$85,000 |
| Project managers | $95,000-$140,000 |

## Contributing Factors

- Tighter immigration policy adding more upward pressure on wages
- Aging workforce accelerating retirement wave
- Skills mismatch between available workers and industry needs
- Competition from other sectors (data centers, manufacturing)

## Industry Response

- Increased focus on prefabrication and modular construction to reduce on-site labor needs
- Robotics and automation deployment expanding (market valued at $1.30 billion in 2026)
- More projects shifting labor into factory settings
- Investment in training and apprenticeship programs

## Impact on Bidding

- Labor shortages making bidding riskier by 2026
- Contractors building larger contingencies into bids
- ConstructConnect warns of significant bidding risk from labor availability
