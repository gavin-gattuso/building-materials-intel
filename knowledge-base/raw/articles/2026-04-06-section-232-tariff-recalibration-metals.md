# April 6 Tariff Recalibration: New Rules for Steel, Aluminum, and Copper Items

## Key Details
- Effective **April 6, 2026**: updated Section 232 tariff rules
- Goods made **almost entirely of aluminum, steel, or copper** now face **50% tariff on the full item value**
- Previously, some finished/semi-finished products avoided the full tariff rate
- This closes loopholes where finished products with high metal content were classified outside Section 232
- Core tariff rates remain: **50%** on steel, aluminum, and copper (UK steel at 25%)

## What Changed
- **Annex I-A** maintains 50% duty on core commodities (coils, sheets, bars)
- New rules expand coverage to **downstream products**: finished metal items, fabricated components, assembled goods
- This catches construction products that are primarily metal but were previously imported as "finished goods" at lower tariff rates
- Examples: metal building components, prefabricated steel structures, aluminum curtain wall systems, copper electrical assemblies

## Construction Industry Impact
- The expanded tariff coverage means **more construction products** will be subject to the 50% rate
- Previously, some fabricated steel and aluminum building components were imported at lower rates
- Metal building manufacturers and facade contractors will see imported component costs rise
- Electrical equipment (switchgear, panels, transformers) made primarily of copper also affected

## Analysis
- This recalibration represents a further tightening of the tariff wall around the US construction supply chain
- It benefits domestic manufacturers of metal building products while raising costs for contractors who sourced fabricated components internationally
- For building materials: domestic metal building manufacturers, window/door producers, and electrical equipment makers gain competitive advantage; international sourcing becomes even more expensive
- The timing -- during the spring construction season -- means the cost impact will be felt immediately on active projects
- Combined with the Iran-driven energy cost shock, this creates a "double squeeze" on construction costs in Q2 2026
