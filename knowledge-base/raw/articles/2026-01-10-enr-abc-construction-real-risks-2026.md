# ABC: Construction Industry to Face 'Real Risks' in 2026

## Key Risks Identified

### Material Cost Escalation
- Tariffs driving double-digit price increases in metals (aluminum, steel, copper)
- Nonresidential construction input prices rising at 7.1% annualized rate
- Margin compression as input costs outpace bid price increases

### Labor Constraints
- 349,000 workers needed in 2026 (down from 439,000 in 2025)
- Decline reflects cyclical slowdown, not structural improvement
- Wages rising 6-8% annually, adding to project costs
- Immigration enforcement adding further pressure

### Policy Uncertainty
- Tariff regime creating unpredictable cost environment
- Surface transportation bill renewal needed
- Interest rate path uncertain (Fed held rates in January)

### Demand Risks
- Consumer confidence at 12-year low could dampen private-sector investment
- Housing permits declining (leading indicator)
- Office market vacancy remains elevated, restraining CRE construction

## Positive Factors

- IIJA spending reaching peak disbursement
- Data center and energy infrastructure demand strong
- Construction employment gained 33,000 jobs in January
- Construction lending standards approaching neutral
- Housing shortage of 3.7 million units provides long-term demand floor

## ENR Analysis

The construction industry faces a complex outlook: strong public-sector demand from IIJA and CHIPS Act projects offset by private-sector headwinds from tariffs, high costs, and consumer pessimism. The industry's ability to navigate these cross-currents will determine whether 2026 delivers growth or stagnation.
