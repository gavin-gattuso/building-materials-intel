# US Cement Tariffs Add $5-$10/Ton to Import Costs; Supply Gaps Emerge in Northern States

## Key Details
- **25% tariff** on cement imports from Canada and Mexico effective April 5, 2025
- **Subsequent adjustment**: Reduced to 10% across-the-board; Canada and Mexico exempted from certain tariffs
- **China tariff**: Increased to 145%
- **10% time-limited tariff**: 150 days, set to expire July 2026 (does not affect Canada/Mexico or existing tariffs)
- **U.S. imports ~20%** of total cement consumption
- **Canada and Mexico supply 27%** of U.S. cement imports (~7% of total U.S. consumption)
- **2023 import volumes**: 5 million metric tons from Canada; 2 million metric tons from Mexico
- **Cost impact**: Tariffs add $5-$10/ton for importers; overall market cost increase of ~1.86%
- **Regional impact**: Northern states (New York, Washington) hardest hit; Canadian imports account for up to 36% of regional consumption
- **Construction cost impact**: Aggregate construction costs estimated to rise ~8% under current tariff conditions
- **Material-specific tariff impact**: 5-25% depending on material type

## Analysis
- Domestic cement producers (CRH, Martin Marietta's remaining cement operations) benefit from tariff-driven import substitution
- CEMEX is uniquely positioned as both a domestic U.S. producer and a Mexican exporter, creating complex dynamics for its business
- Taiheiyo Cement (via CalPortland) could benefit as a domestic U.S. producer, though its Japanese parent may face challenges with the China tariff regime
- The 145% tariff on Chinese cement effectively eliminates that import source, tightening supply further
- Northern states face the most acute supply pressure, potentially driving regional price increases well above the 1.86% national average
- The expiration of the 10% time-limited tariff in July 2026 could create a brief price relief window
- Long-term, tariffs accelerate the trend toward domestic cement capacity investment, benefiting companies with existing U.S. plants
