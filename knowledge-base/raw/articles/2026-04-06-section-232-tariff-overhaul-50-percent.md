# Trump Administration Resets Section 232 Tariffs: 50% on Steel, Tiered System on Derivatives

## Key Details
- **Effective Date**: April 6, 2026
- **Primary Metal Articles** (steel, aluminum, copper): **50% tariff on full customs value**
- **Metal-Heavy Derivatives**: **25% tariff on full value**
- **Industrial/Electrical Grid Equipment**: **15% transitional rate** through 2027
- **Critical Change**: Tariffs now apply to full customs value of all covered articles, not just metal content
- **Previous System**: Based on metal-content valuation, which allowed lower duty exposure
- **Impact on Imports**: Foreign import share of U.S. finished steel market dropped from ~25% to ~14%
- **Imports Down**: 40% year-over-year decline in steel imports
- **New Product Categories**: Department of Commerce added 407 product categories to steel/aluminum tariffs
- **Domestic Price Effect**: HRC steel prices reached $1,074/short ton in early April 2026
- **Winners**: Nucor, Steel Dynamics, Cleveland-Cliffs transitioned from "price-takers to price-makers"
- **Downstream Impact**: Appliance, automotive, and construction manufacturers face sudden cost spikes

## Analysis
- The shift to full-value assessment is the most significant structural change to Section 232 since the tariffs were first imposed, as it closes loopholes where importers could minimize duties by undervaluing metal content
- With imports down 40% and domestic mills controlling pricing, HRC steel above $1,000/ton represents a structural floor that benefits all domestic producers
- For building materials companies that consume steel (HVAC, structural steel, metal roofing), these tariffs represent a significant cost headwind that will likely be passed through to construction costs
- The tiered system (50%/25%/15%) creates a cascading tariff structure that protects the entire steel value chain, from raw steel to semi-finished products
- This policy environment is the most favorable for domestic steel producers since the original Section 232 tariffs were imposed in 2018
