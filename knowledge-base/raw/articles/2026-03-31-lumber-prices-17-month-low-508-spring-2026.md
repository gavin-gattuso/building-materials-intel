# Lumber Prices Hit 17-Month Low of $508.50 in March 2026; Spring Season Outlook Uncertain

## Key Details
- Lumber futures fell to $508.50 per 1,000 board feet in March 2026, a 17-month low
- Down ~18% from January 2026 high of $618.50
- May 2026 futures trading just over $580 in late February; subsequently fell further
- BC mill closures and ongoing US-Canada tariff dispute tightening supply heading into spring
- Canada's industrial carbon tax: $110/tonne effective January 1, 2026 (up 37.5% from $80)
- Seasonal patterns typically favor spring price increases as construction activity picks up
- February housing data weakened, causing earlier restocking flows to fade
- Falling volumes and open interest amplified price decline
- Lumber remains one of the most volatile commodities in the markets

## Analysis
- The March low of $508.50 is well below breakeven levels for most Canadian mills and many higher-cost US Southern mills, suggesting current prices are unsustainable long-term without further permanent capacity exits.
- The failure of the January rally to sustain through spring season launch is concerning and suggests demand weakness may be more structural than seasonal.
- The supply-side response (BC closures, curtailments) should eventually create a floor, but the timeline for price recovery remains uncertain.
- For lumber producers, Q1 2026 will likely show continued losses; profitability recovery requires lumber prices above $550-600 for most operations.
- The carbon tax increase to $110/tonne adds approximately CAD $15-20 per thousand board feet to Canadian mill costs, further widening the gap between Canadian and US Southern production economics.
