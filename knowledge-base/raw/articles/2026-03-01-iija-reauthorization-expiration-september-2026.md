# IIJA Expires September 30, 2026: $131B Remaining, $2.3B Rescinded, Reauthorization Fight Ahead

## Key Details
- IIJA authorization expires **September 30, 2026**
- Five-year program covering fiscal years 2022-2026: **$567 billion** in Highway Trust Fund and General Fund resources
- Through December 2025: states committed **$249 billion** in highway/bridge formula funds to **113,000+ projects**
- Of $711.8B total IIJA funds: **$580.6 billion (82%)** became available FY2022-FY2025
- Remaining **$131.2 billion (18%)** will become available for obligation in FY2026
- FY2026 spending bills rescinded **$2.3 billion** in IIJA allocations
- Including **$879 million** from NEVI (National Electric Vehicle Infrastructure) program
- Core formula programs for highways, bridges, transit, and water survived rescissions intact

## Reauthorization Status
- Congressional committees have begun preliminary work but **no bill has been introduced**
- If continued at IIJA spending levels: **$102 billion/year** going out vs. Highway Trust Fund revenue -- creating a **$58 billion/year deficit**
- Analysts consider it "unlikely that new reauthorization language will be ready for October 1, 2026"
- Congress will likely need to enact one or more short-term **extensions** of current funding levels

## Post-IIJA Risk
- Without reauthorization, programs fall back to **pre-IIJA authorization levels**
- This would represent a significant reduction in federal infrastructure spending
- State DOTs planning based on current funding levels would face disruption

## Analysis
- The IIJA expiration is the single most significant policy risk for infrastructure-exposed building materials companies in 2026
- Companies like Vulcan Materials, Martin Marietta, CRH, and Summit Materials derive significant revenue from highway and bridge construction
- The $131.2 billion becoming available in FY2026 means peak disbursement activity may occur this year -- a near-term positive
- However, the lack of reauthorization progress creates uncertainty for project planning beyond FY2026
- The $2.3 billion rescission signals political willingness to redirect IIJA funds, adding another layer of uncertainty
- For building materials: enjoy the peak spending in 2026 but prepare for potential funding disruption in FY2027 if Congress fails to act
