# Iran War and Hormuz Closure Create Largest Oil Supply Disruption in History

## Key Details
- Brent crude surged to **$120/barrel** at peak (up ~90% from early-year ~$63/barrel levels)
- Brent settled at **$94/barrel** as of March 9, up approximately **50% from January**
- US gas prices hit **$4.00/gallon** on March 31 -- a **30% surge**
- Strait of Hormuz blockaded -- carries approximately **20% of seaborne crude oil and LNG**
- IEA described it as potentially the **largest supply disruption in history**
- LNG prices in Japan/South Korea up **48%**
- Kerosene-based products (diesel, jet fuel) prices **more than doubled**

## Construction-Specific Impacts
- **Diesel prices:** directly increase equipment operating costs and material transport costs
- **Asphalt:** petroleum-derived, directly affected by crude price increases
- **Plastics and insulation:** petrochemical-based construction products see immediate cost pass-through
- **Energy-intensive materials:** cement, glass, bricks, and steel manufacturing costs surge with natural gas/electricity prices
- **Material transport:** aggregates, steel, and prefabricated components all face higher shipping costs

## Price Forecast
- Brent expected to remain above **$95/barrel** through May 2026
- Forecast to fall below **$80/barrel** in Q3 2026 and to approximately **$70/barrel** by year-end
- Assumes conflict de-escalation and partial Hormuz reopening

## Analysis
- The oil shock compounds the tariff-driven material cost increases already pressuring the construction industry
- Combined effect: construction input prices climbing at a **12.6% annualized rate** in early 2026
- The energy shock hits construction through multiple channels simultaneously: fuel for equipment, energy for manufacturing, transport costs, and raw material inputs
- For building materials: asphalt producers, insulation manufacturers, and any company with energy-intensive production face acute margin pressure
- Infrastructure projects (road paving, bridge construction) are especially exposed to both diesel and asphalt price increases
- The crisis introduces stagflation risk: higher costs + potentially slower economic growth
