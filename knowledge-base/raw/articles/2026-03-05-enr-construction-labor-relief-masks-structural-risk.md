# ENR: Construction's Labor 'Relief' Masks Structural Risk as Demand Cools

## Key Thesis

The apparent easing in construction labor demand (349,000 net new workers needed in 2026, down from prior years) masks deeper structural risks that will persist regardless of cyclical conditions.

## Employment Data

- Construction average hourly earnings: **$39.70/hour** (mid-2025)
- Year-over-year wage growth: **3.7%**
- Projected 2026 construction salary growth: **8-12%**
- Broader US economy wage growth: **3.5-4%**
- Construction wages rising at roughly **2-3x** the national average

## Structural Workforce Challenges

### Demographics
- **20%** of construction workforce over age 55
- More than **half** of 349,000 workers needed in 2026 are retiree replacements
- The smaller labor demand figure reflects cyclical slowdown, not workforce improvement

### Immigration Policy
- Tighter immigration policy has added upward pressure on wages
- Construction historically relies on immigrant labor for entry-level positions
- Policy changes constraining the available labor pool

### Skills Mismatch
- Available workers often lack skills needed for modern construction
- Technology adoption (robotics, digital tools) requires new skill sets
- Training pipeline insufficient to meet industry needs

## Implications

- Bidding risk increases as labor availability remains uncertain
- Contractors building larger contingencies into project bids
- Prefabrication and modular construction gaining traction as labor-saving strategies
- Construction robotics market growing from **$1.30 billion** (2026) to projected **$11.14 billion** (2040)
