# Building Materials Are Getting Closer to Doubling as Batteries

## Technology Overview

MIT researchers have developed **electron-conducting carbon concrete (ec3)** -- a form of concrete that combines cement, water, and ultra-fine carbon black nanoparticles to form a conductive internal network, enabling the concrete to function as a supercapacitor.

## Key Breakthrough

- Energy storage capacity increased by nearly **10x** compared to the 2023 version
- Previously: would have required **45 cubic meters** of concrete to power an average home for a day
- Now: only **5 cubic meters** of concrete needed for the same daily energy needs
- Carbon black conducts electricity and forms long, branching "wire" networks as cement hardens

## Advantages Over Traditional Batteries

- Unlike batteries, supercapacitors **do not degrade** over time or lose charge capacity
- Does not require expensive or ethically questionable materials (no cobalt, lithium)
- Carbon black is **inexpensive**, adding little to the cost of making concrete
- Combines structural and energy-storage functions in one material

## Potential Applications

- Parking spaces and roads that could **charge electric vehicles**
- Homes that can operate **fully off the grid**
- Infrastructure that doubles as energy storage (bridges, foundations, road barriers)
- Data centers with built-in energy resilience

## Implications for Building Products Industry

This represents a potential paradigm shift where building materials serve dual structural/energy functions. If commercialized, it could create entirely new product categories for concrete producers and transform how buildings integrate with the energy grid. Construction robotics market valued at **$1.30 billion** in 2026, projected to reach **$11.14 billion** by 2040.
