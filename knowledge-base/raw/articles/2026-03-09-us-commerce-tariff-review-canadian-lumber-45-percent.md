# US Tariffs on Canadian Softwood Lumber Exceed 45% for Most Producers; New Review Launched

## Key Details
- Total US import taxes on most Canadian softwood producers: 45.16%
- Commerce Department anti-dumping and countervailing duties: 35.16% for most producers
- Section 232 tariff (since October 14, 2025): 10% on all global softwood timber/lumber imports
- Company-specific total tariff rates:
  - Canfor: 57.59% (highest among major producers)
  - West Fraser: 36.47%
  - Interfor: anti-dumping duty 20.56% + countervailing duty 14.63%
- US imported ~$7 billion in softwood lumber covered by Section 232 in 2024; ~75% ($5.2B) from Canada
- New administrative review formally announced March 9, 2026; final results expected by January 31, 2027
- January 1, 2026: tariffs reached 30% for upholstered wooden products, 50% for kitchen cabinets/vanities (delayed to Jan 2027 by presidential proclamation)
- Canada's industrial carbon tax: $110/tonne effective January 1, 2026 (up 37.5% from $80)
- Canada's three largest producers collectively operate more sawmills in the US than in Canada

## Analysis
- The 45%+ combined tariff rate is effectively a trade barrier that makes Canadian softwood lumber uncompetitive in the US market at current lumber prices.
- Canfor's 57.59% rate is particularly punitive and explains the company's aggressive mill closure program and U.S. production shift.
- The ongoing tariff review (results expected Jan 2027) introduces another year of policy uncertainty that discourages investment and new production.
- Canada's $110/tonne carbon tax adds another layer of cost disadvantage for Canadian mills, compounding the US tariff burden.
- The structural shift of Canadian producers operating more US than Canadian mills is a multi-decade trend that will reshape North American lumber supply chains permanently.
