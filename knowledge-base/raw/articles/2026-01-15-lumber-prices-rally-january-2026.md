# Lumber Prices Rally 17.5% in January Before Reversing Course Through March

## Key Details - January Rally
- Lumber futures rallied **17.5%** to a high of **$618.50/MBF** on January 21, 2026
- Rally fueled by mortgage rate dip to **6.06%** on January 15 (lowest in 3+ years)
- Demand expectations improved on seasonal restocking
- National average framing lumber: approximately **$872/MBF**
- Year-over-year lumber costs up approximately **13%**

## February Reversal
- Futures fell toward **$590/MBF**, retreating from January highs
- US housing data weakened, dampening demand expectations
- Earlier restocking flows faded
- Dropped below $590/MBF to a nearly four-week low

## March Decline
- Lumber hit a **17-month low** of **$508.50/MBF** in March 2026
- Lost **6.46%** in the preceding four weeks
- Down **13.91%** over the trailing 12 months
- Unusual for lumber, which typically reaches annual highs March-May

## Tariff Context
- Canadian lumber tariffs remain in effect at elevated rates
- Despite tariffs, weak demand is overpowering tariff-driven price support
- Lumber and plywood "flat compared to last year" -- first sustained stabilization since pandemic

## Analysis
- The January-to-March price trajectory (rally then sharp decline) tells the story of a housing market that briefly showed optimism before reality set in
- The March low of $508.50 is significant -- it suggests residential construction demand is genuinely weak, not just seasonally depressed
- For building materials: lumber price weakness is actually margin-positive for homebuilders (lower input costs) but signals weak volume demand
- The divergence between lumber (declining) and metals (surging on tariffs) creates a split market for building materials
- Builders using wood-frame construction are better positioned cost-wise than those relying on steel/aluminum-intensive designs
