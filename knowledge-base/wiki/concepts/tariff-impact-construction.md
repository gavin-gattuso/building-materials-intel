# Tariff Impact on Construction Materials

## Overview
The Trump administration's use of Section 232 tariffs has created the most significant cost shock to the U.S. construction industry since the post-COVID supply chain crisis. As of April 2026, tariffs on key construction inputs have reached historically high levels, fundamentally altering project economics and forcing industry-wide contract restructuring.

## Current Tariff Rates (As of April 2, 2026)
| Material | Tariff Rate |
|----------|-------------|
| Steel, Aluminum, Copper | 50% |
| Steel/Aluminum/Copper derivatives | 25% |
| Industrial/electrical equipment (containing metals) | 15% |
| Foreign products using American metals | 10% |
| Softwood lumber and timber | 10% |
| Lumber derivatives | 25% |
| Mexican and Canadian cement | 25% |
| Global baseline (expires July 2026) | 10% |

## Cost Impact
- Construction input prices surged 12.6% (annualized) in Jan-Feb 2026
- Aluminum prices up ~40% YoY in the U.S.
- Metal molding and trim prices up ~50% YoY
- Estimated $30B added to residential construction investment costs
- ENR Building Cost Index up 4.2% YoY

## Industry Response
- AGC issued urgent guidance to update all contracts with price escalation provisions
- Contractors using ConsensusDocs 200.1 Material Price Escalation Amendment
- Owners beginning to delay or cancel projects when quotes exceed budgets

## Forward Risks
- Additional tariffs under investigation: critical minerals, robotics, semiconductors, heavy trucks
- Global baseline tariff (10%) set to expire July 2026 — extension uncertain
- Domestic materials not price-safe due to demand-driven inflation

## Relevance to Semi-Annual Report
This is a central theme for the report. Tariffs are the single largest cost driver in 2026, affecting every material category and every project type. The report should track both the rate levels and the second-order effects (project delays, margin compression, contract restructuring).
