# SCOTUS Tariff Ruling (February 2026)

## Overview
On February 20, 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court issued a landmark 6-3 decision in *Learning Resources Inc. v. Trump*, striking down the President's authority to impose tariffs under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA). While the ruling removed the legal basis for the administration's reciprocal tariff program, the practical relief for the construction industry was limited because tariffs on the most consequential materials (steel, aluminum, copper, lumber, cement) remained in place under separate authorities.

## The Ruling
- **Case**: Learning Resources Inc. v. Trump
- **Decision**: 6-3
- **Held**: The President is NOT authorized to impose tariffs under IEEPA
- **Effect**: Rejected Trump's claimed authority for reciprocal tariffs against the rest of the world
- **Removed**: Legal basis for much of the administration's 2025 trade program

## Administration Response: Section 122 Replacement
- Within hours of the ruling, the White House invoked **Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974**
- Imposed 10% ad valorem surcharge on most imports effective February 24, 2026
- Section 122 authorizes a surcharge of up to 15% for 150 days unless Congress acts
- Later increased to 15%
- All existing tariffs on key construction materials remained in place

## Tariff Rates Still in Effect Post-Ruling
| Material | Rate | Authority |
|----------|------|-----------|
| Steel | Up to 50% | Section 232 |
| Aluminum | Up to 50% | Section 232 |
| Copper products | 50% | Section 232 |
| Canadian/Mexican cement | 25% | Separate authority |
| Section 122 global surcharge | 10-15% | Trade Act of 1974 |
| Softwood lumber | 10-25% | Separate authority |

## Impact on Construction
- "Modest but meaningful reduction in materials price escalation" for specialty equipment, HVAC, electrical systems and fixtures (per Anirban Basu, ABC chief economist)
- However, tariffs on lumber, steel, aluminum, and copper remain -- the major cost drivers for infrastructure, industrial, and multifamily construction
- Net relief to the construction industry is limited
- NAHB estimates tariff actions add approximately $10,900 to cost of a typical new home
- AGC warned members unlikely to see refunds for materials purchased during the past year

## Budget Impact
- Administration's tariffs projected to raise ~$1.3 trillion over 2026-2035
- Net dynamic revenue (accounting for slower growth): ~$1.1 trillion

## Relevance to Semi-Annual Report
The SCOTUS ruling is symbolically important but practically limited for construction. The key takeaway is that the most impactful tariffs (steel, aluminum, copper at 50%; lumber; cement at 25%) survived the ruling. The Section 122 replacement adds a new layer of tariff complexity. For the report, the ruling should be framed as a legal milestone that did not meaningfully alter the construction cost trajectory.
