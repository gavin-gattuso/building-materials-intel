# Strait of Hormuz Crisis (2026)

## Overview
On February 28, 2026, joint US-Israeli airstrikes against Iranian leadership and military infrastructure (Operation Epic Fury) triggered the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the world's most critical oil chokepoint. The crisis caused the largest disruption to energy supply since the 1970s, with oil prices spiking from ~$78/bbl to a peak of $126/bbl. The cascading effects on construction costs have been devastating, compounding existing tariff pressures into a "perfect storm" for the industry.

## Scale of Disruption
- The Strait of Hormuz normally carries approximately 20% of the world's crude oil and LNG supplies
- Tanker traffic dropped approximately 70%; over 150 ships anchored outside the strait
- IEA characterized it as the "largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market"
- Blockade also disrupted export of petrochemicals, fertilizer, and plastics raw materials
- Transit times extended by weeks as ships forced to reroute around Africa

## Oil & Energy Price Impact
- Oil prices surged approximately 50% from the start of 2026
- Brent peaked at $126/bbl before settling to ~$111/bbl
- Diesel reached $5.40/gallon by late March, up more than $1.90/gallon since start of 2026
- Natural gas prices already up 10.9% month-over-month in February (pre-conflict)
- A 10% rise in diesel pushes headline CPI up by 0.1%

## Construction Cost Impact
- Input prices for nonresidential construction surged at 12.6% annualized rate in first two months of 2026
- Aluminum jumped nearly 40% in a single year
- Rising oil prices directly elevate fuel costs, asphalt prices, and transportation costs for all building materials
- Cascading failure across multiple supply chains driven by spikes in oil, copper, aluminum, and steel
- Diesel hit $5.45/gallon (per AAA), up 45% since start of conflict

## Industry Response
- Contractors pulling back on fixed-price bids
- Escalation clauses becoming standard in new contracts
- Firms prioritizing increased US sourcing
- Investment in cloud-based supply chain visibility
- Use of formal indexed pricing tied to published cost benchmarks
- AGC urging contract updates amid rising material costs
- Some owners delaying project starts to assess cost trajectory

## Relevance to Semi-Annual Report
The Strait of Hormuz crisis is a defining event for 2026 construction economics. It compounds the tariff shock and labor shortage to create what many in the industry are calling a "perfect storm" for construction costs. The crisis affects every material category through energy and transportation costs and has fundamentally altered the risk calculus for project financing and scheduling.
