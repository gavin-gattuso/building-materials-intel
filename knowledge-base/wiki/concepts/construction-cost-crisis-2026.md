# Construction Cost Crisis 2026

## Overview
The U.S. construction industry faces a "perfect storm" of cost pressures in 2026, driven by the convergence of three major forces: historically high tariffs on construction materials, an energy price shock from the Iran/Strait of Hormuz crisis, and persistent labor shortages. Input prices for nonresidential construction surged at a 12.6% annualized rate in the first two months of 2026 -- one of the sharpest accelerations in recent years.

## The Three Crisis Drivers

### 1. Tariffs (Structural Cost Floor)
| Material | Tariff Rate |
|----------|-------------|
| Steel, Aluminum, Copper | 50% (Section 232) |
| Steel/Aluminum/Copper derivatives | 25% |
| Canadian/Mexican cement | 25% |
| Softwood lumber | 10-25% |
| Global baseline (Section 122) | 10-15% |

- Effective tariff rate: 16.9% (Yale Budget Lab, January 2026)
- Estimated $30B added to residential construction investment costs
- NAHB estimates $10,900 added per typical new home
- SCOTUS struck down IEEPA tariffs (Feb 20) but key materials unaffected

### 2. Energy Shock (Acute Escalator)
| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Oil (peak) | $126/bbl (March 2026) |
| Oil (current) | $112/bbl (April 2026) |
| Diesel | $5.40/gallon (late March) |
| Diesel increase | +$1.90/gallon since start of 2026 |

- Strait of Hormuz closure (Feb 28) triggered largest energy supply disruption since 1970s
- Oil prices surged ~50% from start of 2026
- Directly elevates fuel, asphalt, and transportation costs for all materials

### 3. Labor Shortages (Persistent Constraint)
| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Workers needed (2026) | 349,000 (ABC estimate) |
| Workers needed (2027) | 456,000 |
| Wage growth | +4% YoY (some firms +20%) |
| Wage growth (specific trades) | 8-12% |

- Tighter immigration policy adding pressure
- Industry needs ~499,000 new professionals by 2026 (Deloitte)
- Labor moved into factory/prefab settings as partial mitigation

## Material-Specific Cost Impacts
| Material | Price Movement |
|----------|---------------|
| Steel | +11.9% by end of 2025, accelerating into 2026 |
| Aluminum | +40% YoY |
| Metal molding/trim | +50% YoY |
| Lumber (national avg) | $872/MBF, +13% YoY |
| Concrete/Cement | +4-6% (EPA regulations) + 25% tariff on imports |
| Diesel | $5.40/gal, +$1.90 since Jan |

## Headline Metrics
- Construction input prices: +12.6% annualized (Jan-Feb 2026)
- Building material prices (YoY): +3.5% (largest since Jan 2023)
- ENR Building Cost Index: +4.2% YoY
- Overall project expense increase: 4-6% for US construction firms
- Aggregate construction costs: estimated to rise 8% under current tariff conditions

## Industry Response
- AGC issued urgent guidance to update all contracts with price escalation provisions
- ConsensusDocs 200.1 Material Price Escalation Amendment becoming standard
- Contractors pulling back on fixed-price bids
- Project owners delaying or canceling projects when quotes exceed budgets
- Firms prioritizing increased US sourcing
- Investment in cloud-based supply chain visibility

## Forward Risks
- Additional tariffs under investigation: critical minerals, robotics, semiconductors, heavy trucks
- Global baseline tariff (10-15%) set to expire in ~150 days -- extension uncertain
- Domestic materials not price-safe due to demand-driven inflation
- Data center demand competing for materials and labor
- Potential for further oil price escalation if Strait of Hormuz crisis worsens

## Relevance to Semi-Annual Report
This is the central theme of the H1 2026 report. The construction cost crisis is not a single event but a compounding convergence of three structural forces. The report should track the interaction effects between tariffs, energy, and labor -- and their second-order impacts on project feasibility, contract restructuring, and demand destruction.
