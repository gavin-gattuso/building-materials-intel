# Weyerhaeuser (WY)

## Company Overview

Weyerhaeuser Company is one of the world's largest private owners of timberlands, headquartered in Seattle, Washington. The company is organized as a real estate investment trust (REIT) and operates through three segments: Timberlands, Real Estate, Energy & Natural Resources (formerly Strategic Land Solutions), and Wood Products. Weyerhaeuser owns or controls approximately 10.5 million acres of timberlands in the United States, primarily in the Pacific Northwest and US South.

As a REIT, Weyerhaeuser benefits from a tax-advantaged structure and pays a regular base dividend to shareholders, supplemented by special/variable dividends. The Timberlands segment generates revenue from log sales, while the Wood Products segment produces lumber, engineered wood products (EWP), and other building materials. The Real Estate, Energy & Natural Resources segment capitalizes on the highest and best use of Weyerhaeuser's land holdings through real estate development, conservation sales, and natural resource extraction.

Weyerhaeuser has pursued an active timberlands portfolio optimization strategy, completing over $1.2 billion in high-quality acquisitions and approximately $850 million in non-core divestitures between 2022 and 2025. The company launched a new strategy targeting $1.5 billion of incremental Adjusted EBITDA through 2030.

## Tariff & Trade Exposure

- **Limited Direct Tariff Exposure:** As a US-based timberland REIT, Weyerhaeuser has relatively limited direct tariff exposure compared to Canadian lumber producers
- **Indirect Benefit from Section 232:** Tariffs on imported lumber could benefit domestic producers like Weyerhaeuser by reducing import competition and supporting domestic lumber pricing
- **Log Export Restrictions:** Pacific Northwest log exports to Asia subject to trade dynamics and potential restrictions
- **Wood Products Pricing:** Tariffs on Canadian lumber imports support higher US lumber prices, which benefits Weyerhaeuser's Wood Products segment
- **Input Costs:** Steel and other construction material tariffs could slow housing construction, indirectly reducing lumber demand
