## Overview

The building materials industry serves two fundamentally different end markets -- residential and commercial/institutional construction -- that differ in structural systems, procurement channels, project timelines, code requirements, and cyclical behavior. Understanding which materials flow to which end market, and how the boundary between them shifts, is critical for analyzing company revenue exposure, pricing dynamics, and strategic positioning.

At the broadest level, U.S. construction spending splits roughly 40-45% residential (new + R&R) and 55-60% nonresidential (commercial, institutional, industrial, infrastructure). However, materials intensity varies significantly by segment: a single-family home consumes a different product basket than an office tower, a warehouse, or a hospital. The rise of multifamily construction as a share of total housing (averaging approximately 30-35% of total housing starts in the 2015-2025 period, up from roughly 20% in the 1990s) has blurred the traditional residential/commercial materials divide, as mid-rise and high-rise apartments use commercial-grade structural systems with residential-grade interior finishes.

This article maps the materials consumption profile of each major building type, identifies the product categories that cross segment boundaries, and traces how structural shifts in the construction mix affect individual building materials companies.

## Historical Timeline

| Period | Structural Shift | Materials Impact |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|
| 1950-1970 | Suburban single-family dominance; wood platform framing standardized | Softwood lumber, plywood, and gypsum board become commodity staples |
| 1970-1985 | Energy crises drive first insulation standards; commercial construction booms | Fiberglass insulation scales; curtain wall glass demand rises |
| 1985-2000 | Big-box retail and office park expansion; OSB displaces plywood | Engineered wood products gain share; commercial roofing market grows |
| 2000-2006 | Housing bubble inflates single-family share; McMansion trend increases materials per unit | Lumber, concrete, and roofing volumes reach records; average home size peaks near ~2,500 sq ft |
| 2007-2012 | Residential collapse; commercial follows with 18-24 month lag | Producers with commercial/infrastructure exposure (CRH, steel producers) prove more resilient |
| 2013-2019 | Multifamily renaissance; apartments reach ~35-40% of starts in some years | Concrete, structural steel, and elevator demand rise relative to wood framing |
| 2020-2025 | Single-family rebound (2020-2022), then rate shock; industrial/warehouse construction surges | Lumber whipsaws; structural steel and insulated metal panels see sustained demand; data center construction creates new premium segment |

### Framing Material Market Share by Segment

The choice of primary structural system is the single most consequential materials decision in a building project, as it determines the entire supply chain. Wood, steel, and concrete each dominate different segments:

| Structural System | Single-Family (1-3 stories) | Multifamily Low-Rise (1-4) | Multifamily Mid-Rise (5-8) | Multifamily High-Rise (9+) | Commercial Office | Industrial | Education/Healthcare |
|-------------------|-----------------------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|-------------------|------------|---------------------|
| Wood platform frame | ~90-93% | ~65-75% | ~15-25% | <5% | <5% | <5% | ~10-15% |
| Cold-formed steel frame | ~3-5% | ~10-15% | ~10-15% | ~5-10% | ~10-15% | <5% | ~10-15% |
| Structural steel frame | <2% | ~2-5% | ~15-25% | ~30-40% | ~40-50% | ~65-75% | ~25-35% |

### How Multifamily Blurs the Line

The multifamily sector is the critical bridging segment between residential and commercial materials markets. Its growth over the past decade has created important dynamics:

**Low-rise multifamily (1-4 stories)** uses a materials profile that closely resembles single-family construction: wood framing, asphalt shingle roofing, vinyl or fiber cement siding, residential-grade windows and HVAC. This segment benefits the same suppliers as single-family.

**Mid-rise multifamily (5-8 stories)** represents the transition zone. Many of these projects use a "podium" design: one or two stories of concrete parking structure topped by four to five stories of wood framing ("5-over-1" or "5-over-2" construction). This hybrid approach creates simultaneous demand for:
- Concrete and rebar (podium levels)
- Structural lumber and engineered wood (upper levels)
- Commercial-grade fire-rated assemblies (gypsum, insulation)
- Commercial elevator and MEP systems
- Residential-grade interior finishes (cabinets, countertops, fixtures)

**High-rise multifamily (9+ stories)** is functionally identical to commercial construction from a structural materials perspective: reinforced concrete or structural steel frame, curtain wall glazing, commercial HVAC, and fire-rated assemblies throughout.

### Product Categories That Cross Segments

Certain building products serve both residential and commercial markets with limited differentiation:

| Product | Residential Use | Commercial Use | Segment Sensitivity |
|---------|----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Gypsum board | Interior walls and ceilings; standard 1/2" | Fire-rated assemblies; 5/8" Type X | Low -- demand tracks total construction spending |
| Fiberglass insulation | Wall cavities, attics (R-13 to R-38) | Commercial walls, mechanical insulation | Low to moderate -- codes drive commercial specs |
| Roofing (asphalt shingles) | ~80% of residential roofing market | Minimal (commercial uses single-ply, BUR, metal) | High -- almost purely residential |
| Roofing (single-ply/TPO/EPDM) | Minimal | ~50-60% of commercial roofing | High -- almost purely commercial |
| Concrete | Foundations, driveways, flatwork | Structural, foundations, tilt-up walls | Low -- serves both, but mix design differs |
| Windows (vinyl) | ~60-65% of residential window market | Minimal | High -- residential indicator |
| Windows (aluminum curtain wall) | Minimal (high-rise condos only) | Standard for office, institutional | High -- commercial indicator |
| HVAC (unitary/split systems) | Standard residential | Light commercial | Moderate -- overlaps in light commercial |
| HVAC (large tonnage/VRF) | Minimal | Standard commercial/institutional | High -- commercial indicator |

## Current Context

### Segment Mix Shift: 2019-2026

The construction mix has shifted materially in the 2020s, with implications for building materials demand:

| Construction Segment | 2019 Share of Total | 2025 Share (est.) | Direction | Key Driver |
|---------------------|--------------------|--------------------|-----------|------------|
| Single-family residential | ~22-24% | ~18-20% | Down | Mortgage rate impact on starts |
| Multifamily residential | ~6-8% | ~5-7% | Down | Oversupply in Sun Belt; financing constraints |
| Residential R&R | ~26-28% | ~27-29% | Stable/Up | Aging stock, locked-in homeowners |
| Commercial (office, retail) | ~10-12% | ~8-10% | Down | Remote work; retail restructuring |
| Industrial/manufacturing | ~5-7% | ~10-13% | Up significantly | IRA, CHIPS Act, reshoring |
| Warehouse/distribution | ~4-5% | ~3-4% | Stable/Down | E-commerce buildout maturing; data centers partially in this category |
| Data centers | ~1-2% | ~3-5% | Up significantly | AI infrastructure; hyperscaler expansion |
| Healthcare/education | ~8-10% | ~8-10% | Stable | Demographic-driven; bond-funded |
| Infrastructure (highway, water, transit) | ~12-15% | ~14-17% | Up | IIJA funding acceleration |

### What This Means for Materials Demand

**Winners in the current mix**: Structural steel (Nucor, Steel Dynamics, ArcelorMittal), cement and aggregates (CRH, Vulcan, Martin Marietta, CEMEX, Heidelberg, Holcim), insulated metal panels (Kingspan, Carlisle), high-performance insulation (Owens Corning, Kingspan), pipe and drainage (Advanced Drainage Systems), commercial HVAC (Carrier, Trane, Johnson Controls, Daikin).

**Under pressure in the current mix**: Softwood lumber (Canfor, Interfor, West Fraser, Weyerhaeuser -- though R&R provides a floor), residential windows at the value tier (JELD-WEN), residential-oriented plumbing fixtures (some Masco and Fortune Brands product lines).

**Neutral/mixed**: Gypsum board (broad exposure), roofing (residential shingle replacement remains strong; commercial new construction mixed), residential doors (ASSA ABLOY, JELD-WEN -- new construction down but R&R stable).

## Implications for Building Materials

### Strategic Segmentation Framework

Building materials companies can be mapped on a 2x2 matrix of residential vs commercial exposure and new construction vs R&R exposure. This framework reveals fundamentally different business models:

| Quadrant | Characteristics | Example Companies | Cyclical Profile |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Residential New + R&R | Broad housing exposure; volume-driven | Builders FirstSource, UFP Industries | Moderate -- R&R stabilizes |
| Residential New-Heavy | Highly cyclical; start-dependent | Lumber producers (Canfor, Interfor, West Fraser) | High -- leveraged to housing starts |
| Commercial/Infrastructure | Project-driven; longer lead times | CRH, Vulcan, Martin Marietta, Nucor | Moderate -- backlog smooths cycles |
| Cross-Segment | Diversified product serving both | Owens Corning, Saint-Gobain, Carlisle | Lower -- natural hedge |

### Key Analytical Insights

1. **Companies with balanced residential/commercial exposure** (Owens Corning, Saint-Gobain, CRH) tend to exhibit lower earnings volatility than pure-play residential or commercial names. Owens Corning's three-segment model (Roofing/Insulation/Composites, with the 2024 Masonite acquisition adding Doors) exemplifies deliberate diversification.

2. **The multifamily cycle is a swing factor** for materials that sit at the residential-commercial boundary. When multifamily construction peaked in 2022-2023 (~580-600K annualized starts), it meaningfully boosted demand for both wood framing (low-rise) and concrete/steel (mid/high-rise). The pullback in 2024-2025 (~380-440K range) has reduced demand across both material systems.

3. **Industrial and data center construction** are structurally different from traditional commercial: they use extremely high volumes of concrete, structural steel, electrical infrastructure, and specialized HVAC, but minimal interior finish materials. This partially explains why the total construction spending increase from industrial/data center has not proportionally benefited all building products categories.

4. **Code-driven upgrades create cross-segment tailwinds**: Regardless of whether the end market is residential or commercial, tightening energy codes (IECC 2021/2024, ASHRAE 90.1 updates) are increasing the insulation content, window performance, and HVAC efficiency required per building. This is a structural volume and ASP driver for Owens Corning, Kingspan, AGC, Carrier, Trane, and Daikin across both segments.

5. **Material substitution risk is segment-dependent**: Mass timber (CLT) is an emerging substitution threat to concrete and steel in mid-rise commercial and multifamily (roughly 4-12 stories), but has negligible impact on single-family wood framing or heavy industrial construction. Cold-formed steel framing has gained incremental share in low-rise multifamily and light commercial but has not materially penetrated single-family despite decades of promotion.
