# Housing Cycles 1970-2025

## Overview

U.S. housing construction is among the most cyclical sectors in the economy. Since 1970, the industry has completed approximately seven full boom-bust cycles, each driven by a distinct combination of interest rate movements, credit availability, demographic shifts, and macroeconomic conditions. Understanding these cycles is essential for any building materials strategist because residential construction accounts for roughly 40-45% of total U.S. construction spending and is the single largest demand driver for lumber, gypsum, roofing, insulation, and most other building products.

Housing starts -- the number of privately-owned housing units on which construction has begun -- serve as the primary cyclical indicator. The long-run average from 1970 through 2025 is approximately 1.4-1.5 million annualized units. Cycles have ranged from peaks above 2.0 million units (1972, 2005-2006) to troughs below 500,000 units (2009). The typical peak-to-trough decline lasts 2-4 years, while recovery phases tend to be longer and more gradual, often spanning 4-7 years.

Single-family starts exhibit greater cyclical amplitude than multifamily starts. Single-family construction is more sensitive to mortgage rates and consumer confidence, while multifamily is driven more by rental market fundamentals, cap rates, and institutional capital flows. This distinction matters for materials companies because the product mix differs substantially between the two segments.

## Historical Timeline

### 1970s: Inflation, Rate Shocks, and Two Sharp Cycles

The 1970s produced two distinct housing cycles, both compressed and violent by historical standards. The first cycle peaked in early 1972 at approximately 2.36 million annualized starts -- a record that stood for over three decades. The Nixon-era credit controls and the 1973 oil embargo triggered a sharp decline to roughly 1.0 million starts by early 1975. Recovery was swift, with starts climbing back above 2.0 million by 1977-1978, only to be crushed again by the Volcker rate hikes. By 1981-1982, starts had fallen below 1.0 million units.

### 1980s: The S&L Boom and Bust

The early 1980s trough gave way to a sustained recovery as inflation was tamed and rates gradually declined from their peaks. Housing starts recovered to approximately 1.7-1.8 million by 1983-1984, supported by deregulation of savings and loan institutions. The S&L crisis of the late 1980s, combined with the 1990-1991 recession, pushed starts down to approximately 1.0 million by early 1991. The overbuilding in certain Sun Belt markets during the S&L era created regional distress that presaged the more severe national crisis two decades later.

### 1990s: The Long Expansion

The 1990s represented a period of unusual stability. Starts fluctuated in a relatively narrow band between 1.2 and 1.6 million units throughout the decade. The combination of moderate interest rates (7-8% on 30-year mortgages), steady employment growth, and the aging of Baby Boomers into peak homebuying years created sustained but not excessive demand. This period lulled many participants into underestimating the potential for extreme cyclical swings.

### 2000-2006: The Bubble Inflates

The post-2001 easing cycle, combined with the proliferation of subprime and Alt-A mortgage products, securitization, and speculative demand, drove the most extreme housing boom in modern U.S. history. Single-family starts surged from approximately 1.2 million in 2000 to a peak of roughly 1.72 million in January 2006. Total starts (including multifamily) reached approximately 2.07 million annualized in early 2006. Homeownership rates reached a record 69.2% in 2004.

### 2006-2011: The Great Housing Crash

The bust was unprecedented in both depth and duration. Total housing starts fell from 2.07 million (January 2006) to a trough of approximately 478,000 (April 2009) -- a decline of roughly 77%. Single-family starts bottomed near 350,000 annualized units, a level not seen since Census Bureau records began in 1959. The recovery was painfully slow: starts did not sustainably exceed 1.0 million until 2014, and the 1.5 million threshold was not crossed until 2020-2021.

### 2012-2019: The Slow Recovery

The post-GFC recovery was the slowest housing recovery on record. Tight lending standards, impaired household balance sheets, elevated student debt burdens on Millennials, and chronic lot and labor shortages all constrained the pace of new construction. Annual starts averaged roughly 1.1-1.2 million from 2014-2019, consistently undershooting the approximately 1.5 million units per year that demographic models suggested were needed. This underbuilding created a cumulative housing deficit estimated at 3-5 million units by various analyses.

### 2020-2025: The COVID Whipsaw

COVID-19 initially crashed starts to approximately 930,000 annualized in April 2020, but the recovery was remarkably swift. By early 2021, starts had surged above 1.7 million, driven by remote work migration, historically low mortgage rates (below 3%), and a flight to suburban and exurban housing. The Fed's aggressive rate hikes beginning in March 2022 pushed 30-year mortgage rates from approximately 3.2% to above 7% by late 2022, creating a severe affordability shock. Single-family starts declined approximately 25-30% from their 2021-2022 peaks before stabilizing. The "rate lock-in" effect -- where existing homeowners with sub-4% mortgages refused to sell -- constrained existing home inventory and partially supported new construction demand through 2024-2025.

### Leading Indicators

The following indicators typically lead housing starts by 3-12 months:

- **Mortgage purchase applications** (MBA Weekly Survey): 2-4 month lead
- **NAHB Housing Market Index (HMI)**: 1-3 month lead; readings below 50 signal contraction
- **Building permits**: 1-2 month lead by definition (permits precede starts)
- **Mortgage rate direction**: Rate increases typically manifest in starts data within 3-6 months
- **Existing home inventory months-supply**: Rising above 6 months signals weakening demand
- **Consumer confidence (Conference Board)**: 3-6 month lead on purchase decisions

### Structural Shifts Across Cycles

1. **Single-family share has declined**: From approximately 75-80% of starts in the 1970s-1980s to approximately 65-70% in recent cycles, reflecting urbanization and affordability constraints favoring multifamily.
2. **Geographic concentration has increased**: The Sun Belt (TX, FL, AZ, GA, NC, SC, TN) now accounts for a disproportionate share of new starts relative to the 1970s-1980s.
3. **Average home size peaked and is declining**: From approximately 2,660 sq ft in 2015 to approximately 2,200-2,300 sq ft by 2024-2025, affecting per-unit materials consumption.
4. **Labor constraints have become structural**: The construction labor force has not recovered to pre-2007 levels in per-capita terms, limiting the pace of any boom phase.

## Current Context

As of early 2026, housing starts are running in the range of approximately 1.3-1.5 million annualized units, below the estimated long-run demand of 1.5-1.7 million units driven by household formation and replacement needs. The cumulative housing deficit from 2008-2019 underbuilding remains only partially addressed. Mortgage rates in the mid-6% to low-7% range continue to constrain affordability, but the rate lock-in effect supports new construction as the primary source of available inventory in many markets.

Key factors for the cycle outlook:

- **Demographic tailwind**: The Millennial cohort (born ~1981-1996) is now in peak household formation and first-time homebuying years, providing a structural demand floor.
- **Immigration**: Net immigration has contributed significantly to household formation, adding an estimated 400,000+ housing units of annual demand in recent years.
- **Affordability ceiling**: At current rate and price levels, roughly 30-35% of households can afford a median-priced new home, compared to approximately 50%+ historically. This caps the upside of any boom.
- **Builder strategies**: Major public homebuilders have adapted with mortgage rate buydowns, smaller floor plans, and geographic shifts to lower-cost markets.

## Implications for Building Materials

1. **Lumber and wood products** see the most direct and immediate impact from single-family starts changes, with demand shifting within 1-2 months of starts data.
2. **Cement and aggregates** have a longer lag (3-6 months) due to site preparation and foundation timelines, and benefit from both residential and infrastructure spending.
3. **Roofing, insulation, and gypsum** are heavily weighted to single-family and are among the last materials installed, creating a 4-8 month lag from starts to shipments.
4. **The repair and remodel channel** acts as a partial buffer during downturns, as existing homeowners invest in current homes when they cannot or choose not to move. R&R spending typically declines 10-20% in recessions versus 30-60%+ for new construction.
5. **Geographic exposure matters enormously**: Companies with heavy Sun Belt exposure (Builders FirstSource, UFP Industries) are more leveraged to starts than those with diversified geographic and end-market exposure (CRH, Vulcan Materials).

## Data Sources

- **U.S. Census Bureau**: New Residential Construction (Housing Starts), monthly release. Primary data source for all starts figures.
- **FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data)**: Series HOUST (Total Starts), HOUST1F (Single-Family), HOUST5F (5+ Units). Historical data back to 1959.
- **NAHB (National Association of Home Builders)**: Housing Market Index (HMI), Housing Opportunity Index, builder survey data.
- **Mortgage Bankers Association (MBA)**: Weekly Mortgage Applications Survey, purchase and refinance indices.
- **National Association of Realtors (NAR)**: Existing home sales, inventory months-supply, median prices.
- **Freddie Mac**: Primary Mortgage Market Survey (PMMS), weekly 30-year and 15-year conventional mortgage rate averages.
- **Joint Center for Housing Studies, Harvard University**: Annual "State of the Nation's Housing" report -- comprehensive analysis of housing market conditions and trends.
- **U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics**: Construction employment data (CES series), residential building materials PPI.
- **Conference Board**: Consumer Confidence Index, used as a leading indicator for housing demand.
