## Overview

Federal infrastructure legislation is the single largest demand driver for "heavy-side" building materials -- cement, aggregates, ready-mix concrete, structural steel, rebar, asphalt, and pipe. The U.S. federal government has authorized surface transportation programs through a series of multi-year bills since the Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956 created the Interstate Highway System. Each authorization cycle sets funding levels that flow through state Departments of Transportation (DOTs), metropolitan planning organizations (MPOs), and federal agencies, ultimately translating into material purchase orders with a characteristic 2-4 year lag between authorization and peak spending.

Understanding the distinction between authorization, appropriation, and actual spending is critical for building materials demand forecasting. Authorization establishes maximum funding levels and program structures. Appropriation allocates actual dollars (sometimes below authorized levels). Obligation occurs when funds are committed to specific projects. Outlays (actual spending) follow as construction progresses. The lag between a bill's passage and peak material consumption is typically 18-36 months for highway and bridge projects, and 3-5 years for major capital projects like transit systems or water infrastructure.

The 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA, also called the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law or BIL) represented the largest single infrastructure authorization in U.S. history, with approximately $550 billion in new spending above baseline levels over five years. This law is currently in its peak spending years (2025-2027) and is the dominant federal demand driver for heavy-side materials.

### Authorization vs. Appropriation vs. Spending: The Lag Effect

The relationship between legislation and material demand is not instantaneous. A simplified timeline:

| Phase | Typical Duration After Authorization | Activity |
|-------|--------------------------------------|----------|
| Rulemaking & guidance | 6-18 months | Federal agencies issue implementation rules, formula allocations, grant application guidance |
| State/local programming | 12-24 months | DOTs incorporate new funds into STIPs (State Transportation Improvement Programs); project selection |
| Environmental review & design | 12-36 months | NEPA compliance, engineering design, right-of-way acquisition (for new projects) |
| Obligation & bidding | 18-36 months | Funds obligated to specific contracts; construction bids let |
| Construction & material consumption | 24-60 months | Peak material demand; projects under construction |

For IIJA specifically: the bill was signed in November 2021. Formula funds began flowing in FY2022. Competitive grant programs saw first awards in late 2022 and 2023. Peak construction activity and material consumption from IIJA funding is estimated to occur in calendar years 2025-2028, with a long tail extending to 2030+ for major capital projects.

## Key Patterns

### Heavy-Side vs. Light-Side Material Demand

Infrastructure spending disproportionately benefits "heavy-side" materials -- those used in horizontal and civil construction:

**Heavy-side materials (high infrastructure sensitivity)**:
- **Cement and ready-mix concrete**: Infrastructure accounts for approximately 30-35% of U.S. cement consumption. Highway and bridge projects are the single largest end-use segment. A 20% increase in highway spending translates to roughly a 6-7% increase in total cement demand, all else equal.
- **Aggregates (crushed stone, sand, gravel)**: Infrastructure consumes approximately 40-50% of U.S. aggregate production. Road base, asphalt aggregate, and concrete aggregate are primary end uses. Vulcan Materials and Martin Marietta derive approximately 45-55% of revenue from infrastructure-related end markets.
- **Structural steel and rebar**: Bridge construction and repair, transit facilities, and water/wastewater infrastructure are significant steel demand drivers. Rebar consumption for public infrastructure is estimated at 20-30% of total U.S. rebar demand.
- **Asphalt**: Highway resurfacing and new construction consume roughly 85% of U.S. asphalt production.
- **Pipe (concrete, steel, PVC, HDPE)**: Water and wastewater infrastructure spending directly drives pipe demand. Advanced Drainage Systems is a primary beneficiary of stormwater and drainage infrastructure spending.

**Light-side materials (low infrastructure sensitivity)**:
- Windows, doors, siding, roofing, interior finishes, HVAC equipment, and plumbing fixtures are primarily driven by residential and commercial building construction, with minimal direct infrastructure exposure. Companies like JELD-WEN, Masco, Fortune Brands, Carrier, and Trane Technologies have limited direct infrastructure demand exposure.

### State DOT Spending Patterns

Federal infrastructure funds are primarily distributed through formula programs to state DOTs, which administer the majority of highway and bridge spending. Key dynamics:

- **Federal share**: Typically 80% federal / 20% state match for Interstate projects; 80/20 for NHS; varying ratios for other programs. States must appropriate matching funds, which can constrain spending if state budgets are tight.
- **Obligation authority**: Congress sets annual obligation ceilings that control the pace of spending regardless of authorization levels. Historically, obligation authority has occasionally been set below authorized levels, creating a gap between promised and actual spending.
- **Geographic concentration**: The top 10 states by highway spending (Texas, California, Florida, New York, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Illinois, Georgia, North Carolina, Michigan) account for approximately 50-55% of total federal highway obligations. Material demand impacts are correspondingly concentrated.
- **Seasonality**: Public construction spending is highly seasonal in northern states, with peak activity May-October. This creates pronounced seasonal demand patterns for cement, aggregates, and asphalt in approximately 60% of the U.S. market by volume.

### The "Cliff" Problem

A persistent pattern in U.S. infrastructure policy is the expiration or near-expiration of authorization bills, creating uncertainty that chills state DOT project pipelines. Between SAFETEA-LU's expiration in 2009 and the FAST Act's passage in 2015, Congress passed approximately 35 short-term extensions, preventing long-term project planning and depressing material demand below potential. IIJA's five-year authorization (FY2022-2026) provides relative certainty through 2026, but the question of reauthorization will become a market-moving issue beginning in late 2025 and intensifying through 2026.

If IIJA is not reauthorized or extended at comparable funding levels, the "cliff" effect could reduce federal highway obligations by 30-40% from peak IIJA levels, translating to a material decline in cement, aggregate, and asphalt demand beginning in 2027-2028.

### Complementary Legislation

IIJA does not operate in isolation. Several other federal programs contribute to infrastructure-related material demand:

- **Inflation Reduction Act (2022)**: ~$370 billion for clean energy, including grid infrastructure, EV charging stations, building energy efficiency. Material impacts primarily through electrical infrastructure (steel, copper, concrete for substations and transmission) and commercial building retrofits.
- **CHIPS and Science Act (2022)**: ~$52 billion for semiconductor manufacturing. Fab construction is extraordinarily material-intensive (a single advanced fab requires ~$10-20 billion in construction, consuming large volumes of structural steel, concrete, specialty glass, and cleanroom materials).
- **Annual appropriations**: Defense construction (military base facilities), VA hospital construction, federal building GSA projects, and Army Corps of Engineers water projects all contribute incremental demand outside the surface transportation framework.

## Current Context

As of early 2026, the infrastructure spending environment is characterized by:

**IIJA peak spending underway**: Federal Highway Administration data indicates that IIJA obligation rates accelerated significantly through 2023-2025. State DOTs have obligated the majority of formula highway funds through FY2025, and competitive grant programs (INFRA, MEGA, Bridge Investment Program, RAISE) have awarded billions in discretionary funds now reaching the construction phase. The consensus among industry analysts is that IIJA-driven material demand is at or approaching its peak in 2025-2026.

**Reauthorization uncertainty looming**: IIJA's authorization period ends at the close of FY2026 (September 30, 2026). Congressional attention to surface transportation reauthorization has been limited as of early 2026, raising the prospect of short-term extensions rather than a comprehensive successor bill. For building materials companies, this uncertainty requires scenario planning for both continued elevated spending and a potential funding cliff.

**Highway Trust Fund solvency**: The Highway Trust Fund (HTF), which finances the majority of federal surface transportation spending, has faced chronic revenue shortfalls since the federal gasoline tax was last increased in 1993 (to $0.184/gallon). EV adoption further erodes HTF revenue. IIJA addressed the gap with approximately $118 billion in general fund transfers, but long-term solvency requires either a gas tax increase (politically difficult), vehicle-miles-traveled (VMT) fee (pilot programs underway), or continued general fund subsidies.

**Buy America requirements tightening**: IIJA included strengthened Buy America provisions requiring that iron, steel, manufactured products, and construction materials used in federally funded infrastructure be produced in the United States. The Build America, Buy America Act (BABA) within IIJA expanded these requirements beyond transportation to all federal infrastructure programs. This benefits domestic producers (Nucor, Steel Dynamics, Vulcan Materials, Martin Marietta, CRH's U.S. operations) but has created supply chain challenges and cost premiums for some specialized materials.

**State-level infrastructure investment**: Many states have independently increased infrastructure investment through fuel tax increases, bond issuances, toll revenue, and dedicated sales taxes. States including California, Texas, Virginia, Michigan, and Illinois have enacted multi-billion-dollar transportation packages that supplement federal funding. These state programs provide a partial buffer against potential federal funding cliffs.

## Implications for Building Materials

1. **Aggregates and cement producers face a 2025-2027 demand peak followed by uncertainty**: Vulcan Materials, Martin Marietta, CRH, CEMEX, and Heidelberg Materials (U.S. operations) are currently benefiting from peak IIJA spending. However, capacity utilization rates above 85-90% in some regions are constraining volume growth. The post-IIJA demand outlook is the key strategic question for these companies.

2. **Steel producers benefit from both volume and Buy America**: Nucor, Steel Dynamics, and other domestic steelmakers benefit from IIJA spending directly (rebar, structural steel, plate for bridges) and indirectly through Buy America requirements that limit import competition on federally funded projects. The combination of Section 232 tariffs and Buy America creates a double layer of domestic preference.

3. **Pipe manufacturers ride water infrastructure tailwind**: IIJA allocated approximately $55 billion for water and wastewater infrastructure, the largest federal investment in water systems in history. Advanced Drainage Systems, along with PVC and ductile iron pipe producers, face a multi-year demand tailwind that extends beyond the surface transportation authorization timeline because water projects have longer development cycles.

4. **The authorization-to-spending lag requires patience**: Building materials companies and their investors must understand that infrastructure bill passage does not equal immediate material demand. The 18-36 month lag means that a potential IIJA successor bill passed in 2027 would not generate peak material demand until 2029-2030. Demand forecasting must account for this pipeline dynamic.

5. **Regional exposure varies significantly**: Companies with operations concentrated in high-spending states (Texas, California, Florida, major Midwest/Southeast states) will capture disproportionate IIJA benefits. Vulcan Materials (Southeast, Southwest focus), Martin Marietta (Southeast, Midwest), and CRH (broad U.S. footprint) have varying regional exposure profiles that affect their IIJA demand capture.

6. **Light-side building materials companies have limited direct exposure**: Infrastructure legislation has minimal direct demand impact on companies focused on residential and commercial building products (JELD-WEN, Masco, Fortune Brands, Owens Corning, Carrier, Trane Technologies). However, indirect effects through economic multipliers (infrastructure construction employment, wage growth, housing demand near infrastructure improvements) provide modest secondary benefits.
