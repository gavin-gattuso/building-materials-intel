## Overview

Repair and remodel (R&R) activity represents approximately 45-50% of total residential construction spending in the United States, making it one of the single largest demand drivers for building materials. In years when new construction contracts sharply (2008-2011, 2023-2024), R&R spending proves more resilient, often declining only 5-15% versus 40-60% drops in new residential starts. This counter-cyclical stability makes R&R exposure a strategically valuable characteristic for building materials companies.

The R&R market is driven by fundamentally different factors than new construction. While housing starts respond primarily to mortgage rates, household formation, and builder confidence, R&R spending is governed by housing stock age, home equity levels, homeowner tenure (length of time in current home), disaster/insurance repair cycles, and aging-in-place requirements. The market is split between professional contractors ("Pro" channel) and do-it-yourself homeowners ("DIY" channel), each with distinct product preferences, distribution pathways, and price sensitivities.

For building materials companies tracked in this intelligence platform, R&R exposure ranges from dominant (Masco, Fortune Brands -- roughly 60-75% of revenue tied to R&R) to minimal (Vulcan Materials, Martin Marietta -- almost entirely new construction and infrastructure). Understanding this split is essential for revenue forecasting, channel strategy analysis, and competitive positioning assessment.

## Historical Timeline

| Period | R&R Market Development | Key Driver |
|--------|----------------------|------------|
| 1970-1985 | R&R emerges as a recognized market segment; home centers (Home Depot founded 1978, Lowe's expands nationally) create dedicated retail channel | Baby Boomers purchase first homes; energy crisis drives weatherization |
| 1985-1995 | R&R spending roughly doubles in nominal terms; professional contractor segment formalizes | Aging 1950s-1970s housing stock reaches 20-40 year replacement cycle for roofs, windows, HVAC |
| 1995-2006 | Home equity extraction fuels discretionary remodeling boom; kitchen and bath remodels surge | Rising home prices create "wealth effect"; HELOC originations reach estimated $350-400B+ annually by 2005-2006 |
| 2007-2011 | R&R spending declines approximately 20-25% in real terms -- painful but far less than the ~60-70% collapse in new construction | Home equity evaporates; HELOC originations collapse; discretionary projects deferred; maintenance spending continues |
| 2012-2019 | Gradual recovery; R&R spending returns to pre-crisis levels by approximately 2017; Pro channel gains share over DIY | Home values recover; aging stock continues to require maintenance; millennial renters drive landlord R&R spending |
| 2020-2022 | Pandemic triggers largest R&R boom in decades; spending surges an estimated 20-30% in nominal terms 2020-2022 | Work-from-home drives home office/outdoor living investment; stimulus payments; supply chain disruptions inflate costs; inability to move (low inventory) redirects spending to current home |
| 2023-2025 | Normalization from pandemic highs; spending declines approximately 5-12% in real terms from 2022 peak but remains well above 2019 levels | Rate lock-in effect keeps homeowners in place (supporting R&R over moving); discretionary projects pull back; insurance/disaster repair remains strong |

### Housing Stock Age as the Fundamental Driver

The median age of the U.S. housing stock has been rising steadily, creating a structural tailwind for replacement and maintenance spending. As of the 2023 American Housing Survey, approximately:

| Housing Stock Age Cohort | Approximate Share of Occupied Units | Key R&R Implications |
|--------------------------|-------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Built 2010 or later | ~12-14% | Minimal R&R needs; warranty repairs only |
| Built 2000-2009 | ~14-16% | Entering first major replacement cycle (HVAC, water heaters, exterior paint) |
| Built 1990-1999 | ~13-15% | Second replacement cycle; roofs, windows, siding reaching end of life |
| Built 1980-1989 | ~12-14% | Major systems replacement; potential energy efficiency upgrades |
| Built 1970-1979 | ~13-15% | Extensive renovation candidate; plumbing, electrical, insulation, roofing all potentially due |
| Built 1960-1969 | ~9-11% | Often requires comprehensive updating; kitchen/bath, structural repairs |
| Built 1950-1959 | ~8-10% | Foundation, structural, and systems issues common; aging-in-place modifications |
| Built before 1950 | ~13-16% | Continuous maintenance required; lead paint, asbestos abatement; historic preservation |

*Source: U.S. Census Bureau, American Housing Survey (AHS). Shares are approximate based on 2021-2023 survey data.*

**Critical observation**: Roughly 55-65% of the U.S. housing stock was built before 1990, meaning the majority of homes are now 35+ years old. Most major building components have useful lives of 15-30 years (roofing: 20-30 years; HVAC: 15-20 years; windows: 20-30 years; siding: 20-40 years depending on material), placing the bulk of the housing stock in active replacement territory.

### Pro vs DIY Market Share Evolution

The split between professional contractor and DIY spending has shifted meaningfully over the past two decades:

| Year | Pro Share (est.) | DIY Share (est.) | Key Factors |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------|
| 2000 | ~55-58% | ~42-45% | DIY culture strong; home center expansion at peak |
| 2005 | ~53-56% | ~44-47% | HGTV effect peaks; home equity finances both channels |
| 2010 | ~56-60% | ~40-44% | Recession suppresses discretionary DIY; necessary repairs favor Pro |
| 2015 | ~58-62% | ~38-42% | Millennials less DIY-inclined; dual-income households value time over savings |
| 2020 | ~52-56% | ~44-48% | Pandemic reverses trend: lockdowns, free time, stimulus, Pro contractor unavailability |
| 2022 | ~54-58% | ~42-46% | Pandemic DIY enthusiasm fading; labor market normalization |
| 2025 (est.) | ~60-64% | ~36-40% | Long-term Pro trend reasserts; aging homeowner population; complexity of energy-efficiency retrofits favors Pro |

*Sources: JCHS Harvard estimates; Home Depot and Lowe's investor disclosures (Pro vs DIY sales mix); NAHB Remodeling Market Index; industry consultant estimates.*

**Drivers of the long-term Pro shift**:
1. **Demographic aging**: Homeowners aged 55+ are the largest R&R spending cohort by dollar volume but increasingly unable or unwilling to perform physical labor themselves
2. **Project complexity**: Energy efficiency retrofits, smart home integration, and code compliance increase the technical skill required
3. **Permitting and liability**: More jurisdictions require permits and licensed contractors for electrical, plumbing, and structural work
4. **Time scarcity**: Dual-income households and gig economy participation reduce available DIY time
5. **Skill erosion**: Younger generations report lower confidence in basic home repair skills compared to Baby Boomers

### Insurance and Storm Repair Cycles

Weather-driven repair constitutes an estimated 10-15% of total R&R spending in a typical year, but can spike dramatically in years with major hurricane, wildfire, or severe storm events. This spending is largely non-discretionary and funded by insurance proceeds, making it the most resilient sub-segment of R&R.

| Event Type | Estimated Annual R&R Impact | Primary Materials Affected | Geographic Concentration |
|------------|----------------------------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Hurricanes (Atlantic/Gulf) | ~$15-50B in peak years; ~$5-10B in quiet years | Roofing (asphalt shingles, metal), windows/doors (impact-rated), siding, lumber, drywall | Florida, Texas Gulf, Carolinas, Louisiana |
| Severe convective storms (hail, tornado, wind) | ~$15-30B annually (growing trend) | Roofing (dominant), siding, gutters, fencing | Central U.S. (TX, OK, CO, MN, NE), Southeast |
| Wildfires | ~$5-15B in severe years; ~$1-3B in moderate years | Complete rebuild (all materials); fire-resistant materials for adjacent properties | California, Pacific Northwest, Mountain West |
| Winter storms / freeze events | ~$3-8B annually | Plumbing (pipe burst repair), HVAC, insulation, drywall | Variable; 2021 Texas freeze was exceptional (~$15-20B) |
| Flooding (non-hurricane) | ~$5-10B annually | Drywall, flooring, insulation, HVAC, foundation repair | Midwest, Southeast, coastal areas |

*Sources: NOAA National Centers for Environmental Information (Billion-Dollar Disasters database); Insurance Information Institute; industry estimates. Figures include insured and uninsured losses allocated to building repair.*

**Key insight for building materials analysts**: Storm repair demand creates a "call option" in the earnings of roofing-exposed companies. Owens Corning's Roofing segment, for example, has historically seen volume and pricing uplift in the 6-18 months following major hail or hurricane seasons, as repair demand absorbs regional inventory and contractors replenish supply.

### The LIRA Index and Forecasting R&R

The Leading Indicator of Remodeling Activity (LIRA), published quarterly by the Joint Center for Housing Studies at Harvard University, is the most widely cited forward-looking metric for R&R spending. It projects homeowner improvement spending four quarters ahead based on:

- Building permits for residential improvements
- Home sales (existing and new)
- House price appreciation (FHFA index)
- Residential investment as a share of GDP
- Consumer confidence

**Historical LIRA performance**: The index has generally tracked actual R&R spending direction with reasonable accuracy but has tended to underestimate volatility in both directions. It failed to fully anticipate the magnitude of the pandemic-era R&R surge and has arguably been too pessimistic about the subsequent normalization (actual spending has remained above LIRA projections in several recent quarters).

**Limitations**: LIRA captures broad market direction but does not distinguish between Pro and DIY, between discretionary and non-discretionary spending, or between different product categories. Analysts should supplement LIRA with company-specific sell-through data and distributor inventory reports.

## Current Context

### 2024-2026: The "Lock-In" Effect Era

The defining feature of the current R&R market is the mortgage rate lock-in effect. With an estimated 80-85% of outstanding U.S. mortgages carrying rates below 5% (and roughly 60% below 4%), homeowners face an enormous financial disincentive to sell and buy a new home at prevailing rates of 6.5-7.5%. This has two consequences for R&R:

1. **Positive**: Homeowners who cannot move are investing in their current homes. Kitchen and bath remodels, home office upgrades, aging-in-place modifications, and energy efficiency improvements all benefit.

2. **Negative**: Existing home sales (running at approximately 4.0-4.3M annually in 2024, well below the 5.0-5.5M historical norm) have declined, and home sales are a traditional trigger for R&R spending -- both by sellers preparing homes for market and buyers renovating after purchase.

The net effect appears modestly negative in the near term (the home-sale effect dominates the lock-in reinvestment effect), but the long-term floor under R&R remains high due to housing stock aging.

### Aging-in-Place: The Emerging Structural Driver

The aging of the Baby Boomer generation (born 1946-1964, now aged 62-80) is creating a large and growing market for accessibility-focused home modifications:

| Aging-in-Place Category | Estimated Annual Market Size (2025) | Growth Trajectory | Key Products |
|------------------------|--------------------------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Bathroom accessibility | ~$8-12B | Growing ~5-8% annually | Walk-in tubs, grab bars, curbless showers, comfort-height toilets |
| Mobility modifications | ~$3-5B | Growing ~6-10% annually | Stairlifts, ramps, wider doorways (JELD-WEN, ASSA ABLOY), lever handles |
| Lighting and electrical | ~$2-4B | Growing ~4-6% annually | LED upgrades, smart switches, whole-home generators |
| Smart home / safety | ~$2-4B | Growing ~10-15% annually | Smart locks (ASSA ABLOY), video doorbells, leak detectors, medical alert integration |
| Kitchen modifications | ~$4-6B | Growing ~4-6% annually | Lower countertops, pull-out shelving, touchless faucets (Masco, Fortune Brands) |

*Sources: JCHS Harvard ("Housing America's Older Adults"); AARP surveys; NAHB aging-in-place specialist data; industry estimates.*

**By 2030**, the 65+ population is projected to exceed 73 million (Census Bureau), with approximately 80% expressing a preference to age in their current home. This creates a multi-decade structural tailwind for specific building products categories, particularly plumbing fixtures, door hardware, and bathroom products.

### Strategic Insights

1. **R&R is the stability anchor** in building materials portfolios. Companies with 40%+ R&R exposure (Masco, Fortune Brands, Owens Corning's Roofing segment) have historically demonstrated lower peak-to-trough earnings declines during housing downturns. During the 2007-2010 cycle, Masco's revenue declined roughly 30-35% versus 55-65% declines for new-construction-focused peers.

2. **The Pro channel shift rewards distribution relationships**. As Pro gains share over DIY, the importance of two-step distribution (manufacturer to distributor/dealer to contractor) increases relative to one-step retail (manufacturer to Home Depot/Lowe's to consumer). Companies with strong Pro distribution networks (Builders FirstSource, Ferguson, ABC Supply) are structurally advantaged.

3. **Storm repair creates earnings volatility that is misunderstood by markets**. Roofing companies often see post-storm earnings beats treated by investors as "one-time" events, but the statistical regularity of severe weather in the U.S. means storm repair demand is better modeled as a recurring variable than a true one-off. Owens Corning and Carlisle have both benefited from this pattern.

4. **The aging-in-place opportunity is still under-penetrated**. Despite the demographic certainty of this trend, most building products companies have not yet created dedicated product lines or marketing strategies for aging-in-place. Companies that establish leadership in this niche (Fortune Brands with touchless faucets, ASSA ABLOY with smart/accessible locks, Geberit with accessible bathroom systems) have a multi-decade growth runway.

5. **R&R spending is a lagging indicator of home prices**, with an estimated 4-8 quarter lag. The home price appreciation of 2020-2023 is still supporting R&R spending in 2025-2026, even as the rate of appreciation has moderated. A meaningful home price decline (>10% nationally) would be the primary downside risk to R&R forecasts.

6. **Energy efficiency mandates are converting discretionary R&R into non-discretionary**. Programs like New York's Local Law 97 (building emissions limits), California's Title 24 updates, and federal tax credits (25C, Inflation Reduction Act) for heat pumps, insulation, and windows are creating regulatory and incentive-driven R&R demand that is less sensitive to the economic cycle.
