## Overview

Steel is the structural backbone of commercial, industrial, and infrastructure construction. The US construction sector consumes approximately 40-50 million tons of steel annually, representing roughly 40-45% of total domestic steel consumption. This makes construction the single largest end market for steel, ahead of automotive (~25%) and energy/machinery (~15-20%). The US construction steel market is valued at approximately $50-70 billion annually, though this figure fluctuates significantly with pricing cycles.

The US steel industry has undergone a profound structural transformation over the past four decades: the shift from integrated blast furnace/basic oxygen furnace (BOF) production to electric arc furnace (EAF) mini-mills. This transition -- driven by economics, technology, and the availability of scrap -- has fundamentally reshaped the competitive landscape, cost structures, and environmental profile of American steelmaking. Today, EAF production accounts for approximately 70% of US raw steel output, one of the highest shares globally.

For building materials analysts, understanding steel supply dynamics is essential because steel pricing directly impacts the cost of structural framing, rebar, metal decking, roofing, studs, and countless other construction inputs. Steel is also one of the most trade-exposed building materials, with import competition and trade policy (particularly Section 232 tariffs) playing a major role in domestic pricing.

**The EAF revolution**: The shift from BOF to EAF is the most important structural change in US steelmaking. Key drivers:

- **Capital cost**: A greenfield EAF mini-mill costs $1.5-2.5B for 1.5-3.0M ton capacity vs. $5-10B+ for an integrated mill of similar capacity
- **Operating cost**: EAF cash costs of $450-600/ton vs. BOF cash costs of $550-750/ton (varies with scrap and iron ore prices)
- **Flexibility**: EAFs can be started and stopped in hours; blast furnaces require weeks to adjust and are typically run continuously
- **Scrap availability**: The US generates approximately 60-70M tons of ferrous scrap annually, providing abundant, low-cost feedstock
- **Environmental profile**: EAF produces approximately 0.4-0.6 tons of CO2 per ton of steel vs. 1.8-2.2 tons for BOF
- **Product range expansion**: Modern EAFs with advanced metallurgy can now produce flat-rolled products (sheet, plate) previously exclusive to integrated mills. Nucor's entry into flat-rolled in the 1990s-2000s was the pivotal moment.

### EAF vs. BOF Economics for Construction Products

| Factor | EAF Mini-mill | BOF Integrated Mill |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Primary input** | Ferrous scrap (~70-80% of metallic input) + pig iron/DRI | Iron ore + metallurgical coal |
| **Metallic input cost** | $300-450/ton (varies with scrap prices) | $250-400/ton (varies with ore/coal) |
| **Conversion cost** | $100-200/ton | $200-350/ton |
| **Total cash cost** | $450-650/ton | $550-750/ton |
| **Typical EBITDA/ton** | $100-300/ton (highly cyclical) | $50-200/ton (highly cyclical) |
| **Capacity utilization breakeven** | ~55-65% | ~75-85% |
| **Construction products produced** | Rebar, merchant bar, structural shapes, sheet/plate, studs, decking | Plate, structural shapes, sheet, tin plate |
| **Key US producers** | Nucor, Steel Dynamics, CMC, Gerdau | US Steel (Gary, Mon Valley), ArcelorMittal (Burns Harbor, Indiana Harbor) |

**Section 232 impact**: The 25% tariff on steel imports imposed in March 2018 under Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962 (national security justification) has reduced import penetration by approximately 5-8 percentage points from pre-tariff levels. Some trading partners (EU, Japan, UK) have negotiated tariff-rate quotas or exemptions, but the base 25% tariff remains in effect for most countries as of 2025-2026.

The tariff's effects on the construction industry:
- Domestic steel prices are estimated to be 15-25% above world market levels, adding approximately $2,000-5,000 to the steel cost of a typical commercial building
- Import competition has shifted from direct finished product imports to semi-finished imports (slabs, billets) that are processed domestically
- Some construction-specific products (rebar, structural shapes) have seen less import displacement than flat-rolled products because they were already primarily domestically sourced

**Total construction share**: Approximately 40-45% of all US steel consumption goes to construction end uses, making it the largest single end market. This share has been relatively stable over the past two decades, though the mix within construction has shifted toward infrastructure (bridges, highway) and away from commercial buildings during periods of weak office/retail construction.

### Scrap Pricing Dynamics

Ferrous scrap is the critical cost input for EAF steelmakers, representing 60-70% of the metallic charge and approximately 50-60% of total production cost.

Key scrap benchmarks:
- **#1 Busheling**: Clean factory scrap from stamping plants; highest quality; $350-500/gross ton (highly cyclical)
- **#1 Heavy Melt (HMS)**: Obsolete scrap from demolition, appliances, vehicles; $280-420/gross ton
- **Shredded**: Auto bodies and mixed ferrous processed through shredders; $300-450/gross ton
- **Prime/DRI**: Direct reduced iron; used to supplement scrap in EAFs; $350-500/ton

Scrap pricing is influenced by:
1. **Domestic steel production levels**: Higher utilization = higher scrap demand = higher prices
2. **Export demand**: Turkey, Southeast Asia, and other EAF-heavy regions compete for US scrap exports (~15-20M tons/year)
3. **Auto production**: The automotive industry generates ~5-8M tons of prompt scrap annually; auto production slowdowns reduce supply
4. **Demolition activity**: Building and infrastructure demolition generates significant obsolete scrap; countercyclical to new construction
5. **Pig iron and DRI imports**: Approximately 5-8M tons of pig iron and DRI are imported annually as scrap substitutes, primarily from Brazil, Russia (historically), and Trinidad

**Nucor's vertical integration into scrap**: Nucor's 2008 acquisition of David J. Joseph Company (DJJ), one of the largest scrap processors/brokers in North America, was a strategically significant move to secure scrap supply and capture margin at the raw material stage. SDI's OmniSource subsidiary plays a similar role.

## Current Context

### 2025-2026 Market Dynamics

**New EAF capacity wave**: The US steel industry is in the midst of the largest capacity expansion cycle in decades:

| Company | Project | Capacity (M tons) | Products | Status (est.) |
|---------|---------|-------------------|----------|---------------|
| Nucor | West Virginia sheet mill | 3.0 | Flat-rolled | Commissioning 2025-2026 |
| Steel Dynamics | Sinton, TX (Phase 2 expansion) | +0.5-1.0 | Flat-rolled | Operational |
| US Steel | Big River Steel Phase 2 | +1.6 | Flat-rolled | Operational |
| CMC | Arizona 2 micro-mill | 0.5 | Rebar, merchant bar | Operational |
| Nucor | Brandenburg, KY plate mill | 1.2 | Plate | Operational |
| Hybar | Arkansas rebar mill | 0.6 | Rebar | Under construction |

This capacity addition of approximately 8-10M tons (net of closures) will increase domestic supply and potentially put downward pressure on pricing, particularly for flat-rolled products. However, infrastructure spending and industrial construction demand may absorb much of the new capacity.

**Section 232 continuation**: The 25% steel tariff remains in effect with no clear pathway to removal. Both political parties have expressed support for maintaining trade protection for the domestic steel industry, though the mechanisms may evolve (tariff-rate quotas, product exemptions, bilateral deals).

**Nippon Steel / US Steel**: The proposed $14.9B acquisition of US Steel by Nippon Steel (Japan) was blocked by the Biden administration in January 2025 on national security grounds. The transaction remains in legal limbo, with potential restructuring or alternative arrangements possible. The outcome will have significant implications for the future of integrated steelmaking in the US.

**Decarbonization and green steel**: EAF steelmakers are positioning for low-carbon steel demand:
- Nucor and SDI already have significantly lower carbon footprints than integrated producers (~0.4-0.6 tons CO2/ton vs. ~1.8-2.2 tons)
- Buy Clean mandates in federal procurement and state building codes (California AB 2446, GSA requirements) are creating premium demand for low-embodied-carbon steel
- Green steel premiums of $20-50/ton are emerging for verified low-carbon products
- Hydrogen-based DRI (replacing natural gas) is the next frontier; several pilot projects are underway globally, though US projects are in early stages

**Rebar market tightness**: Infrastructure spending is driving strong rebar demand. US rebar consumption is estimated at approximately 8-10M tons annually, with domestic production at roughly 7-8M tons. The gap is filled by imports (primarily from Turkey, which faces Section 232 tariffs) and by domestic mills increasing rebar production. CMC and Nucor are the dominant domestic rebar producers.

## Implications for Building Materials

### For Industry Strategists

1. **EAF dominance is permanent and accelerating**. The remaining US integrated mills (US Steel's Gary Works, Mon Valley; ArcelorMittal's Indiana Harbor, Burns Harbor; Cleveland-Cliffs' operations) face structural cost disadvantages and will continue to lose share. Construction steel supply will be increasingly EAF-sourced.

2. **Scrap availability is the strategic constraint** for EAF expansion. As EAF capacity grows, competition for prime scrap intensifies. Companies with captive scrap sourcing (Nucor/DJJ, SDI/OmniSource) have structural advantages. Watch for scrap price inflation as a leading indicator of EAF cost pressure.

3. **Trade policy creates a domestic price premium** that benefits US steelmakers but raises costs for construction. The 15-25% premium above world prices translates to meaningful cost increases for steel-intensive projects (bridges, high-rises, industrial facilities). Builders and GCs must factor this into project economics.

4. **Infrastructure spending creates a multi-year demand floor** for construction steel products, particularly rebar, structural shapes, and plate. This reduces cyclicality relative to historical patterns and supports investment in capacity dedicated to construction end markets.

5. **Low-carbon steel is becoming a specification requirement**, not just a marketing differentiator. Companies and projects seeking LEED certification, Buy Clean compliance, or corporate sustainability targets are increasingly specifying low-embodied-carbon steel. EAF producers are well-positioned; integrated producers face a structural disadvantage.

### For Analysts

- Track AISI weekly raw steel production and capacity utilization for supply-side indicators
- Monitor Platts/Fastmarkets HRC and rebar price assessments for pricing trends
- Watch ferrous scrap export volumes (Census trade data) for demand competition signals
- Section 232 exclusion requests and tariff modification proposals signal policy direction
- Compare domestic-to-import price spreads for effective tariff impact assessment
- Follow Buy Clean policy developments at federal and state levels for demand signals
