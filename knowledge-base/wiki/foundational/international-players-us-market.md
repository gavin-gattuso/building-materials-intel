## Overview

Foreign-headquartered companies control a remarkably large share of the US building materials market -- an estimated 35-45% of total industry revenue across manufacturing and distribution. This dominance, built through decades of cross-border M&A, reflects the strategic importance of the US construction market (the world's largest by value) and the willingness of European and Asian building materials conglomerates to pay acquisition premiums for access to its growth, scale, and profitability.

The internationalization of US building materials accelerated in the 1990s with European cement companies and has expanded across virtually every product category. Today, companies headquartered in Ireland (CRH), Switzerland (Holcim), Germany (HeidelbergCement/Heidelberg Materials), France (Saint-Gobain), Mexico (Cemex), Japan (Daikin, LIXIL, Sanwa Holdings, Taiheiyo Cement), Sweden (ASSA ABLOY), Germany (Knauf), and Austria (Wienerberger) are all significant players in US building materials. Several of these companies generate 30-60% of their global revenue from North America.

The trend has reached an inflection point: multiple European companies are now restructuring to become US-listed or US-focused entities, recognizing that US capital markets assign higher multiples to North American pure-play building materials businesses than to European-listed diversified conglomerates.

## Historical Timeline

### European Cement Expansion (1988-2005)

The first wave of international entry targeted the US cement and aggregates market, driven by the commodity's local market dynamics and the fragmented ownership structure of US producers.

**Key transactions:**
- **Holcim (Holderbank)** built its US presence through acquisitions of Dundee Cement (1990), Ideal Basic Industries assets, and later St. Lawrence Cement (2001). By 2005, Holcim was the second-largest cement producer in the US.
- **Lafarge** acquired Redland PLC's US operations (1997) and expanded through plant acquisitions and greenfield capacity. Combined with Holcim in 2015.
- **Cemex** made its transformative US entry with the $2.8 billion Southdown acquisition (2000), making it a top-3 US cement producer overnight.
- **HeidelbergCement** acquired Lehigh Cement (part of its Lehigh Hanson subsidiary) and expanded through multiple aggregates acquisitions, becoming the third-largest US aggregates producer.
- **CRH** began its US strategy in the 1970s with small aggregates acquisitions and steadily built a diversified US platform across cement, aggregates, asphalt, and building products. By 2005, CRH was generating over 50% of revenue from North America.
- **Buzzi Unicem** (Italy) acquired Lone Star Cement (2001), gaining a significant US cement position in the Southeast.
- **Taiheiyo Cement** (Japan) acquired CalPortland (2010 -- full acquisition completed in stages from earlier minority stake), operating primarily on the US West Coast.

### European Building Products Expansion (2000-2015)

Following the cement wave, European building products companies expanded into US manufacturing and distribution:

- **Saint-Gobain** built a major US presence through CertainTeed (roofing, siding, insulation -- acquired decades earlier in 1976), then expanded via acquisitions including GCP Applied Technologies (~$2.3 billion, 2021) and numerous bolt-ons in construction chemicals, abrasives, and distribution.
- **ASSA ABLOY** (Sweden) entered the US through the acquisition of Yale locks and expanded aggressively into commercial and residential door hardware, access control, and door systems. US operations now represent approximately 40% of group revenue.
- **Kingspan** (Ireland) expanded its US insulation and building envelope business through multiple acquisitions, targeting the high-performance building segment.
- **Wienerberger** (Austria) entered the US through pipe and clay products, then expanded into plastic piping (Pipelife).
- **Knauf** (Germany) acquired USG Corporation for approximately $7 billion (2018), gaining the leading US gypsum/wallboard position including the iconic Sheetrock brand.

### Asian Companies' US Entry (2005-Present)

Japanese companies have been the most active Asian acquirers in US building materials:

- **Daikin Industries** (Japan) acquired Goodman Global for approximately $3.7 billion (2012), instantly becoming a top-3 US residential HVAC manufacturer. Daikin has since invested heavily in US manufacturing capacity and completed additional bolt-on acquisitions.
- **LIXIL** (Japan) acquired American Standard Brands' kitchen and bath operations, gaining a significant US plumbing fixtures position. LIXIL's US brands include American Standard, GROHE (global), and DXV.
- **Sanwa Holdings** (Japan) entered the US through Overhead Door Corporation, becoming a leading manufacturer of garage doors, commercial doors, and access systems.
- **Taiheiyo Cement** (Japan) completed full ownership of CalPortland, operating cement, aggregates, and ready-mix on the West Coast.
- **AGC Inc.** (Japan, formerly Asahi Glass) maintains US flat glass operations, competing with domestic players in architectural and automotive glass.

### Recent Structural Shifts (2020-Present)

The current period is defined by European companies restructuring their corporate structures to capture the US valuation premium:

- **CRH moved its primary listing to NYSE** (September 2023), becoming the largest building materials company listed in the US. This reflected the reality that approximately 75% of CRH's EBITDA was already generated in North America.
- **Holcim announced the planned separation of its North American business** into a standalone US-listed entity, expected to be one of the largest building materials IPOs in US history.
- **Saint-Gobain** has increasingly weighted its portfolio toward North America through both acquisitions and divestitures of non-core European operations.
- **Heidelberg Materials** rebranded from HeidelbergCement (2023) and continues to invest in US operations while exploring strategic options for non-core assets.

### Motivations for US Market Entry

| Motivation | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Market size | US construction spending exceeds $2 trillion annually, largest single market globally | All entrants |
| Growth premium | US population growth and housing deficit support higher structural growth than Europe or Japan | Daikin, Kingspan |
| Valuation arbitrage | US-listed building materials trade at 12-18x EV/EBITDA vs. 8-12x for European peers | CRH relisting, Holcim spin-off |
| Dollar exposure | Revenue diversification away from euro/yen-denominated home markets | Saint-Gobain, LIXIL |
| Regulatory environment | Less restrictive permitting and labor regulation in many US states vs. Europe | Wienerberger, Kingspan |
| Fragmentation opportunity | US distribution and regional manufacturing remain more fragmented than European equivalents | CRH, ASSA ABLOY |
| Infrastructure spending | IIJA and federal infrastructure programs create multi-year demand visibility | Heidelberg, CRH, Holcim |

## Current Context

**Holcim's North American separation** is the most significant pending transaction. The planned spin-off of Holcim's North American operations (which generated approximately $11 billion in revenue and $2.5+ billion in EBITDA in 2024) into a US-listed entity could create one of the top 5 US-listed building materials companies. The new entity would combine cement, aggregates, ready-mix, roofing, and building envelope products -- a unique cross-category portfolio.

**CRH's continued US bolt-on strategy** shows no signs of slowing. The company completed an estimated $3+ billion in US acquisitions in 2023 alone and has publicly stated its intent to deploy $20-30 billion in capital over the coming years, weighted heavily toward North America. CRH's strategy of acquiring small-to-mid-size aggregates, asphalt, and paving companies in growing US metros creates a highly defensible competitive position.

**Japanese companies are deepening, not widening.** Rather than entering new categories, Daikin, LIXIL, and Sanwa are investing in US manufacturing capacity, distribution networks, and aftermarket service capabilities. Daikin's Houston-based Daikin Texas Technology Park (opened 2017, expanded 2020) is one of the largest HVAC manufacturing facilities in the world.

**Regulatory scrutiny is rising.** The DOJ's review of building materials M&A has intensified, particularly for cement and aggregates where foreign ownership is already high. The failed ASSA ABLOY/Spectrum Brands (hardware and home improvement) deal (2024, DOJ challenge) signals increased antitrust attention on foreign acquirers building dominant positions in concentrated US building materials categories.

**Nearshoring and supply chain resilience** are creating new dynamics. Some US buyers now prefer domestically manufactured products for supply chain reliability, which benefits foreign-owned companies with US manufacturing operations (Daikin, Knauf/USG) over those that import finished products.

## Implications for Building Materials

1. **US building materials is a global industry governed by local dynamics.** Despite high foreign ownership of manufacturing assets, competition remains intensely local in cement, aggregates, and ready-mix due to transport economics. Foreign ownership at the corporate level does not eliminate local market structure -- it overlays global capital allocation and strategic planning onto regional competitive battles.

2. **The relisting/restructuring wave benefits US capital markets.** As CRH, Holcim, and potentially others establish US-listed entities focused on North American operations, the US building materials public equity universe expands, attracting more analyst coverage, index inclusion, and institutional capital. This is a structural positive for sector valuation.

3. **Remaining domestic independents face strategic decisions.** Companies like Vulcan Materials, Martin Marietta, Eagle Materials, and Owens Corning operate in categories where foreign-owned competitors have significant scale. While these companies have strong competitive positions, they face continued acquisition interest from well-capitalized foreign strategic buyers.

4. **Cultural integration remains a challenge.** European and Japanese management styles can clash with US building materials culture, which is often decentralized, entrepreneurial, and relationship-driven. Successful foreign acquirers (CRH, Daikin) have generally maintained local management autonomy while imposing financial discipline and capital allocation frameworks from headquarters.

5. **Currency fluctuations affect deal timing.** A strong dollar (as in 2022-2024) makes US acquisitions more expensive for European buyers but increases the dollar-denominated earnings that attract them. Conversely, a weaker dollar creates acquisition windows. This currency dynamic creates episodic deal clustering that building materials companies and advisors can anticipate.

6. **Geopolitical risk is minimal but non-zero.** Unlike semiconductors or defense, building materials have faced limited "buy American" restrictions. However, the IIJA includes some domestic content preferences, and political sentiment around foreign ownership of critical infrastructure materials (cement, steel) could evolve.
