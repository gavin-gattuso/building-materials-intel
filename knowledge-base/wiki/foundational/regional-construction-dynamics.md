## Overview

U.S. construction activity is not uniformly distributed. Regional dynamics -- driven by population migration, regulatory environments, climate exposure, land availability, and economic base -- create sharply divergent demand profiles for building materials. Understanding these patterns is essential for any strategic assessment of building products companies, whose revenue and margin structures are heavily influenced by where construction occurs, not just how much occurs nationally.

The dominant structural trend of the past three decades has been the Sun Belt migration boom, which has concentrated residential construction permits, infrastructure investment, and materials demand in a band of states stretching from the Carolinas through Texas and into the Mountain West. Simultaneously, Rust Belt and Northeast markets have shifted toward repair and remodel (R&R) and selective infill development, while coastal metros face affordability-driven outmigration and increasingly stringent building codes.

These regional dynamics directly affect the competitive positioning of tracked companies. Aggregates producers like Vulcan Materials and Martin Marietta derive outsized revenue from Sun Belt markets. Lumber demand correlates with single-family starts concentrated in the South. Cement consumption patterns follow infrastructure spending, which increasingly flows to high-growth states.

## Key Patterns

### Sun Belt Migration: The 30-Year Structural Boom

The Sun Belt's construction dominance rests on a self-reinforcing cycle: population growth drives housing demand, which attracts employers, which drives further population growth. According to Census Bureau estimates, the South census region has accounted for approximately 55-60% of total U.S. population growth since 1990 and roughly 50-55% of all residential building permits issued over the same period.

### Rust Belt Stabilization and R&R Pivot

The traditional manufacturing belt (Ohio, Michigan, Pennsylvania, Indiana, Illinois) experienced decades of population stagnation or mild decline, resulting in minimal new residential construction relative to housing stock. However, this region has emerged as disproportionately important for:

- **Repair & remodel spending**: Older housing stock (median age often 50+ years) drives replacement demand for roofing, windows, siding, insulation, and plumbing
- **Industrial/manufacturing construction**: CHIPS Act semiconductor fabs (Ohio), EV battery plants (Michigan, Indiana, Georgia), and reshoring facilities
- **Infrastructure replacement**: Aging bridges, water systems, and roads requiring heavy materials (aggregates, cement, rebar)

### Coastal vs Interior Cost Structures

Building in coastal metros (New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Boston, Seattle) involves fundamentally different cost structures than interior markets, with direct implications for materials selection and volume:

| Cost Factor | Coastal Metro | Interior/Sun Belt | Impact on Materials |
|-------------|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Land cost (% of home value) | ~40-60% | ~15-25% | Drives density; favors concrete/steel over wood |
| Labor cost premium | ~30-50% above national avg | ~0-15% above/below avg | Favors prefabrication, modular construction |
| Regulatory/entitlement timeline | 18-36+ months typical | 3-9 months typical | Reduces overall volume; increases per-unit materials cost |
| Energy code stringency | Generally exceeds IECC by 1-2 cycles | Often adopts IECC baseline | Higher insulation R-values, triple-pane windows, advanced HVAC |
| Seismic/wind requirements | Seismic (West Coast), hurricane (Southeast coast) | Generally lower | Engineered wood, reinforced concrete, impact-rated products |

### Climate-Driven Demand Shifts

Climate exposure increasingly shapes regional materials demand through three mechanisms:

1. **Building code evolution**: Florida's post-Hurricane Andrew building code reforms (1994-2002) created a permanent premium market for impact-resistant windows, doors, and roofing. Similar code tightening is spreading to Gulf Coast states and the Carolinas.

2. **Storm repair cycles**: Hurricane and severe weather events generate episodic but massive materials demand. A major Gulf Coast hurricane can consume an estimated 6-12 months of regional roofing supply. Insurance-driven repairs often specify upgraded materials, creating a ratchet effect on product quality standards.

3. **Energy efficiency mandates**: States like California (Title 24), Washington, and New York (Local Law 97) are mandating building performance standards that drive demand for high-performance insulation (Owens Corning, Kingspan), advanced glazing (AGC, LIXIL), and efficient HVAC systems (Carrier, Trane, Daikin).

## Current Context

### 2024-2026 Regional Dynamics

The current environment features an unusual divergence in regional construction drivers:

**Manufacturing construction boom (Midwest/Southeast)**: IRA and CHIPS Act incentives have driven manufacturing construction spending from roughly $90-100B annually (2019-2021) to an estimated $220-240B range (2024-2025). Key projects include semiconductor fabs in Ohio, Arizona, and Texas; EV battery plants across the Southeast and Midwest; and clean energy manufacturing in Georgia, South Carolina, and elsewhere. This disproportionately benefits cement, ready-mix concrete, structural steel, and heavy aggregates.

**Residential slowdown and partial recovery**: Elevated mortgage rates (fluctuating in the 6.5-7.5% range through much of 2024-2025) have constrained single-family starts below the roughly 1.0-1.1M annual pace that demographic demand would support. Sun Belt markets remain relatively stronger due to continued in-migration, but the gap between Sun Belt and national performance has narrowed.

**Infrastructure spending acceleration**: IIJA (Bipartisan Infrastructure Law) funding has begun flowing into state DOT budgets, with an estimated $150-180B in incremental spending over 2024-2028 for highways, bridges, water systems, and broadband. This benefits aggregates, cement, and pipe (Advanced Drainage Systems, among others) with relatively uniform geographic distribution, though states with larger deferred maintenance backlogs (Northeast, Midwest) may see disproportionate benefit.

### Strategic Takeaways

1. **Sun Belt remains the structural growth engine**, but the margin of outperformance over other regions has narrowed as manufacturing and infrastructure spending boost Midwest and mid-South markets.

2. **Materials companies with infrastructure exposure** (aggregates, cement, pipe) are less dependent on any single regional residential cycle than those focused on new residential construction (lumber, framing components).

3. **Climate adaptation is becoming a demand driver in its own right**, creating premium product categories (impact glass, high-wind-rated roofing, resilient drainage systems) that command higher ASPs and margins.

4. **The Midwest industrial renaissance** (semiconductors, EV batteries, data centers) represents a potential multi-decade demand shift that is still in early stages. Companies with capacity or reserves in Ohio, Indiana, Michigan, and adjacent states are positioned to benefit.

5. **Population growth remains the single best leading indicator** for regional building materials demand, with an approximate 12-24 month lag between net migration data and permit activity.

## Data Sources

- **U.S. Census Bureau**: Building Permits Survey (monthly), Construction Spending (Value of Construction Put in Place, monthly), Population Estimates Program (annual), American Community Survey
- **Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)**: Producer Price Index for construction materials by region, employment data by metro
- **NAHB (National Association of Home Builders)**: Housing Market Index, state-level permit analysis, builder surveys
- **Joint Center for Housing Studies (JCHS), Harvard University**: "The State of the Nation's Housing" (annual), "Improving America's Housing" (biennial R&R analysis)
- **Federal Highway Administration (FHWA)**: Highway Statistics Series, state DOT funding allocations
- **NOAA/NCEI**: Billion-Dollar Weather and Climate Disasters database (storm repair demand proxy)
- **State-level sources**: Individual state DOT and building commission permit databases; Florida Building Commission; California Building Standards Commission
- **Industry associations**: Portland Cement Association (PCA) regional cement consumption; American Institute of Architects (AIA) regional Billings Index; Associated General Contractors (AGC) state-level data
