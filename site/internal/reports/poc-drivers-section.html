Recent Trends & Outlook

Interest and Mortgage Rates

The Federal Reserve shifted from tightening to modest easing in 2025, cutting rates twice and lowering the federal funds target to 3.75–4.00% by November. The 30-year fixed mortgage rate averaged 6.7–6.9% for most of the year before easing to 6.2–6.35%. Despite this, borrowing costs remain high, restraining single-family demand and commercial financing. The Fed's dovish tone suggests further gradual cuts ahead, though stimulative effects will materialize slowly and unevenly.

Impact on Construction: Elevated borrowing costs suppress new single-family starts and delay commercial project commitments. Refinancing remains unattractive, limiting capital recycling.

Implication for Materials & Products: Demand for framing, finishing, and HVAC products softens in residential channels. Infrastructure and institutional projects, less rate-sensitive, provide partial offset.

Material and Energy Costs

After post-pandemic corrections, material and energy costs stabilized through late 2024 and 2025, with the construction materials PPI up only 1.3% YoY. Lumber prices fluctuated between $430–$690 per MBF, settling near $500 as weak demand offset a 35% Canadian tariff. Copper and steel prices plateaued but now face renewed pressure from tariffs raising import duties to 20–25%. Fuel costs stayed steady but remain sensitive to global supply shocks.

Impact on Construction: Stabilized input costs support margin recovery for contractors and manufacturers, but tariff escalation poses a renewed threat to project economics.

Implication for Materials & Products: Expanding tariffs on metals, lumber, and appliances could add $7,500–$10,000 to new home costs, reintroducing volatility and compressing builder margins.
