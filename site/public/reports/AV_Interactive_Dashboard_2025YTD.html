Executive Summary

Performance & Outlook

The US building materials and products sector delivered mixed but broadly resilient results through the first half of 2025. Total US construction spending reached an estimated $2.18 trillion, up roughly 3.8% YoY, driven by continued strength in non-residential categories — particularly data centers, semiconductor fabrication, and public infrastructure — while residential activity remained tepid amid elevated mortgage rates and affordability headwinds. Infrastructure-linked companies outperformed, supported by sustained IIJA, CHIPS Act, and IRA disbursements, while residential-exposed names faced volume headwinds partly offset by pricing discipline and R&R demand stability.

Margins held up better than expected across most segments, with cost control, pricing power, and favorable mix shifts helping to offset input cost volatility from tariff-related disruptions on steel, aluminum, and Canadian lumber. M&A activity accelerated, with aggregate deal sizes rising approximately 24% YoY as companies pursued scale, vertical integration, and geographic diversification.

Key Risks to Monitor

Residential Recovery Fragility

Labor Shortages
Persistent skilled labor shortages and wage pressures risk prolonging project delays.
Tight Lending Standards
Higher equity requirements (~25-30%) constrain starts and private-sector recovery.
Input Cost Volatility
Renewed volatility from tariffs and geopolitical disruption could erode margin gains.
Success Factors: Outperformers will combine cost discipline, pricing strength, and operational agility with investment in supply chain resilience, workforce development, digital transformation, and sustainable product innovation.

Market Context & Outlook

2025 YTD Summary

Through the first three quarters of 2025, the US building materials sector navigated a complex demand landscape defined by divergent subsector performance. Non-residential construction — particularly data centers, semiconductor facilities, and federally funded infrastructure — delivered strong growth, while residential markets remained constrained by persistently elevated mortgage rates and tighter lending conditions.

Mortgage rates averaged 6.7–6.9% through mid-year before easing modestly to 6.2–6.35% by Q3 — still well above levels needed to catalyze meaningful residential recovery.
The Architecture Billings Index contracted for 16 consecutive months through September 2025, signaling ongoing softness in commercial project pipeline formation.
Tariff hikes on steel, aluminum, and Canadian lumber renewed cost volatility and reignited margin pressure across input-dependent segments.

EOY 2025 + 2026 Prospectus

Looking ahead, we expect continued bifurcation between infrastructure/non-residential (supported by IIJA and CHIPS disbursements hitting peak flow) and residential (contingent on Fed rate trajectory and credit availability). Key swing factors for 2026 include the pace and magnitude of rate cuts, tariff policy clarity, and whether the ABI's prolonged sub-50 reading translates into meaningful commercial backlog erosion.

Interest & Mortgage Rates

Interest rates remained the most impactful headwind for the building materials sector in 2025. The Federal Reserve held the federal funds rate at 5.25–5.50% through the first half of the year before initiating modest cuts in Q3, bringing the effective rate to approximately 4.0% by September. The 30-year fixed mortgage rate tracked a delayed response, averaging 6.7–6.9% before easing to 6.2–6.5%. These levels continued to constrain housing affordability and depress new residential activity, particularly for first-time buyers.

Labor Dynamics

Construction employment rose steadily to approximately 8.3 million by mid-2025, reflecting ongoing demand for labor despite macroeconomic uncertainty. However, the sector continued to face acute skilled labor shortages, with job openings remaining volatile — spiking to 310,000 in July 2025 before dropping sharply to 188,000 in August. Wage pressures persisted above broader economy averages, adding to project cost escalation and timeline risk.

US Construction Labor — Total Employment (M) vs. Job Openings (K), Jun 2024–Aug 2025

Material & Energy Costs

Input costs moderated from their 2022 peaks but remained structurally elevated. Construction materials PPI stabilized around 320, while metals retreated from peak levels above 440 to approximately 300. Lumber continued its pattern of high volatility, settling near 270 after extreme swings. Cement and concrete products maintained a steady upward trajectory, reflecting persistent demand and limited supply additions. Petroleum product prices, a key input for transportation and asphalt, declined from mid-2022 peaks but remain above pre-pandemic levels.

Demand Visibility — Residential

New privately owned housing units authorized by building permits remained subdued in 2025, with an estimated 1.45 million total permits (1.02M single-family, 0.43M multi-family). Single-family permits declined 4.7% YoY, while multi-family starts pulled back amid oversupply concerns in select metros and tighter construction lending. Overall permit activity fell 2.3–3.7% from 2024, steeper than initially expected.

Demand Visibility — Nonresidential

The Architecture Billings Index (ABI), a leading indicator for non-residential construction activity, remained in contraction territory throughout 2025. After briefly approaching the 50-point threshold in early 2024, the index resumed its downward trajectory, hitting 43.3 in September 2025. This represents 16 consecutive months below the 50 expansion/contraction baseline, the longest sustained contraction since the 2008–2009 financial crisis.

National Architecture Billings Index (2022–2025, 50 = Expansion/Contraction Baseline)
16 consecutive months below 50 as of September 2025 — the longest sustained contraction since the 2008–2009 financial crisis, signaling pipeline softening for commercial and mixed-use projects in 2026.

Government Infrastructure Spending

Federal infrastructure disbursements provided the strongest positive catalyst for the sector in 2025. Public non-residential construction spend reached an estimated $480 billion, up approximately 9% YoY, driven by accelerating IIJA project awards and CHIPS Act facility construction. Private non-residential spend also expanded, reaching $870 billion, though growth moderated as data center and semiconductor facility pipelines entered execution phases.

Credit Availability & Lending Standards

Credit conditions tightened faster than anticipated in 2025, with regional and national lenders raising underwriting standards across commercial real estate and construction lending. Key developments include:

LTV/LTC Ratios Reduced
Loan-to-value and loan-to-cost ratios reduced across both regional and national lenders, reflecting heightened risk aversion in CRE lending.
Equity Requirements: 25–30%
Sponsor equity requirements raised to approximately 25–30%, up from 15–20% pre-tightening, constraining smaller developers.
Regional Banks Pull Back
Regional lenders pulled back sharpest; larger sponsors still accessing capital through private credit and institutional partnerships.

GDP Growth & Consumer Confidence

US GDP continued to expand in 2025, with the index reaching approximately 122 (2019 = 100), reflecting cumulative real growth of over 22% since pre-pandemic levels. However, consumer sentiment — as measured by the University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment Index — declined to 55, its lowest level since the pandemic trough. This divergence between objective economic output and subjective consumer confidence is one of the most notable features of the current cycle.

US GDP Index vs. U-M Consumer Sentiment (2019–2025, indexed to 100 at 2019)
Why GDP strength is not reflected in sentiment — Lingering inflation effects, declining trust in economic mobility, and increasingly pessimistic survey biases depress sentiment even as GDP grows. For the building sector, this means consumer-facing segments (R&R, retail distribution) may underperform relative to what GDP headline numbers would suggest.

Sector Driver Conclusion

The 2025 market environment presents a clear bifurcation: infrastructure and non-residential tailwinds remain robust, anchored by federal spending programs, while residential and credit-sensitive segments face persistent headwinds from elevated rates, tight lending, and weakening consumer confidence. Companies with balanced exposure across end markets, strong pricing discipline, and cost control capabilities are best positioned to navigate this divergent landscape.

Public Company Performance Snapshot

Across the 35 public companies tracked in this report, H1 2025 results revealed a sector adapting to structural shifts rather than cyclical recovery. Revenue growth was narrowly positive for infrastructure-linked names (CRH +4.8%, Martin Marietta +4.9%, Vulcan +5.0%) but negative for most lumber and steel producers. Margin performance was broadly resilient, with EBITDA margins expanding for a majority of companies despite modest topline headwinds — reflecting the sustained focus on cost discipline, pricing power, and portfolio optimization that has defined the sector since the 2022 inflationary peak.

M&A remained a defining theme. QXO's transformative acquisition of Beacon Roofing Supply drove a dramatic revenue increase but heavily distorted its operating ratios. ASSA ABLOY, Owens Corning, and CRH continued bolt-on acquisition strategies. The aggregate deal value across the tracked universe rose approximately 24% YoY, with infrastructure adjacencies and building envelope companies as the most active targets.

Residential-exposed companies managed through volume declines with margin preservation strategies. Builders FirstSource, JELD-WEN, and Fortune Brands each saw revenue contractions but largely maintained or narrowed EBITDA margin erosion through cost reduction programs, manufacturing footprint optimization, and selective pricing actions.

Positioning for EOY 2025 & Beyond

As the sector enters the final quarter of 2025, strategic positioning depends heavily on end-market exposure and operational flexibility. Companies aligned with secular infrastructure themes — data centers, semiconductor fabrication, renewable energy, and federally funded transportation — continue to benefit from multi-year spending tailwinds. Residential-focused companies must demonstrate pricing discipline, R&R resilience, and cost flexibility to sustain margins through what may be a prolonged recovery timeline.

The ABI's extended contraction poses the most significant forward-looking risk for commercial and mixed-use project formation. While current backlogs remain supportive through mid-2026, new project wins may decelerate unless billings stabilize above 50 in the coming quarters.

Infrastructure-Aligned

Best positioned: companies with exposure to data centers, semiconductor facilities, utilities, and transportation infrastructure. IIJA/CHIPS funding flows peak in 2025–2026.

Residential-Focused

Navigating headwinds through R&R demand stability, efficiency gains, and disciplined pricing. Recovery contingent on rate trajectory and affordability improvements.

ABI Risk — Commercial

Backlog moderation signal for commercial and mixed-use in 2026. 16+ months of sub-50 ABI readings suggest project pipeline thinning ahead.

Trend Continuity & Retrospective

Variances from Expectation

Several key metrics diverged from our earlier 2025 guidance:

Residential Permits
Fell 2.3–3.7% YoY (single-family -4.7%), steeper than the flat-to-modest-decline scenario we projected. Persistent affordability constraints and cautious builder sentiment drove the undershoot.
ABI Contraction
The ABI reached 43.3 in September, extending the sub-50 streak to 16–19 months — deeper and longer than our base case of stabilization at 47–49 by mid-year.
Labor Inflation
Wage growth in construction exceeded broader economy averages by a wider margin than anticipated, adding to project cost escalation.
Credit Conditions
Tightened faster than guidance — equity requirements rose to 25–30% (vs. our 20–25% assumption), and regional bank pullback was more pronounced.

Looking Ahead: Key Variables

Fed Rate Path
The pace and magnitude of 2026 rate cuts will be the single most influential variable for residential recovery. Markets currently price 75–100bps of cuts by end of 2026, but actual delivery remains highly data-dependent.
Tariff Impact
Tariff policy uncertainty on steel, aluminum, and Canadian lumber continues to cloud input cost visibility. Escalation risk remains a key margin headwind for 2026.
